Five questions to ponder

How people actually learn

Three principles that should shape every design decision you make.

Sheena

~1 min. Transition back from break. Set up the mini-lecture: "Before we write learning outcomes, we need to understand how learning actually works — because some of what feels intuitive is wrong."

1. Cognitive load

Working memory is smaller than you think.

  • People can hold ~4 new items at a time
  • Have you heard of the "Curse of Knowledge?"
  • Intrinsic load — the genuine challenge of the material
  • Extraneous load — confusion caused by your design

Your job: reduce extraneous load so learners can focus on what matters. What are some strategies?

~3 min. Pause after explaining and ask: "Where have you seen this — or its opposite — in a training you've attended or run?" Take 1–2 quick responses. Practical strategies: simplify instructions, remove clutter, pre-teach vocabulary, chunk activities into stages.

2. Building long term memory

Memory is built when people pull knowledge out, not when you push it in.

  • Recognising an answer ≠ being able to produce it
  • The struggle of retrieval is the point, not a sign of failure
  • Memorisation is usually not enough. Apply knowledge in a new context → deeper memory
~3 min. Pause and ask: "Where have you seen this?" Take 1–2 responses. The more ways learners retrieve knowledge, the stronger it sticks. Applying it in a new context forces them to look at it from a different angle, building richer mental frameworks.

3. Social learning

People learn by talking, arguing, and building on each other's ideas.

  • Explaining something to someone else deepens your own understanding
  • Peer challenge surfaces gaps you didn't know you had
  • Structure matters — "discuss in groups" is not social learning
~3 min. Pause and ask for examples. The key is structured social learning — clear tasks, defined roles, specific outputs. "Discuss" without structure produces polite nodding, not learning.

Quick show of hands

Who has heard of learning styles?

~1 min. Hands will go up. Then challenge it…

The learning styles myth

If you're an "auditory learner," could you learn to drive a car by listening to someone talk about it?

If you're a "visual learner," could you learn to play guitar by watching videos?

The evidence doesn't support matching instruction to styles.

What works: varying how you engage learners — because different activities build different kinds of understanding.

~2 min. Let the room sit with it for a moment. The point: learning styles are a pervasive myth. Don't waste time trying to categorise learners by "type." Instead, design varied activities because the content demands it, not because learners have fixed preferences.

From outcomes to activities

You've written outcomes that describe what learners will do.

Now you design the activity that gets them there.

An activity is not a topic — it's a thing learners do during the training that lets them practise the skill they need to achieve.

Anelda

~5 min framing. Key message: "Content serves activities, not the other way around. You don't start with 'what should I cover?' — you start with 'what should learners practise?'" Brief mention: well-designed activities can also function as assessment — if a learner completes the activity well, you can see they've achieved the outcome.

Exercise 5: Start Designing an Activity

Phase 1 — Design

15 min · Own-team breakout

Pick one learning outcome from Exercise 4. Design an activity that helps learners achieve it. Or use one you already have

  1. Objective — which outcome does this address?
  2. Instructions — step-by-step, clear enough for another facilitator to run
  3. Output — what do learners produce? (something concrete)
  4. Materials — what do learners need?
  5. Participation — how is everyone involved, not just the confident ones?
  6. Time — how long, broken into stages

Sheena

~2 min setup + 15 min breakout. Remind teams: "You won't get through all six components — that's expected. Prioritise Objective, Instructions, and Output. These three give enough shape to share meaningfully." Teams with 6+ members should split into sub-groups.
