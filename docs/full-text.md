# Part 1: Designing Training for Real-World Impact

# 1. Getting Started: Training as Intervention and System

| Learning Outcomes | Guiding Questions |
|------------------|------------------|
| <ul><li>Describe training as an intervention within a broader system</li><li>Identify key elements of the system your training operates in (actors, resources, constraints, influences)</li><li>Articulate a simple theory of change linking activities to outcomes and impact</li><li>Analyse how context, power, and positionality influence training design and outcomes</li><li>Identify key assumptions and risks that may affect the success of your training</li></ul> | <ul><li>What system does your training sit within, and who are the key actors?</li><li>What change are you trying to contribute to, and how will training support that?</li><li>What assumptions are you making about how change will happen?</li><li>What factors outside your control could affect success?</li><li>How do your role and positionality influence the design and delivery of this training?</li></ul> |

## Why this matters

Many training programmes are designed as events:

- a workshop
- a course
- a set of materials

But training does not exist in isolation.

Training is an intervention in a system. It interacts with institutions, communities, resources, and constraints. Its outcomes depend on much more than what happens during a session.

If you design training without understanding the system it sits in, you risk:

- overestimating impact
- ignoring constraints
- designing something that cannot work in practice

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What should I teach?”*  
    to:  
    *“What change is possible in this system—and how can training contribute?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Training as an intervention"
    Training is a deliberate action intended to contribute to change, but it only works in relation to the wider system around it.

Training may introduce new knowledge, build skills, create opportunities for practice, or shift behaviour. But training alone rarely creates impact. Impact depends on whether learners can apply what they learn, whether the environment supports change, and whether the training is aligned with real needs.

**Systems thinking (practical)**  
A system includes people, resources, relationships, constraints, and external influences. Your training sits within this system. It does not control it.

**Theory of Change**  
A Theory of Change is a simple explanation of how your training is expected to lead to outcomes and impact:

- Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact

It helps you make assumptions visible and identify where things may break down.

**Positionality, roles, and power**  
Training is not neutral. Your role, background, authority, and assumptions shape what is prioritised, whose knowledge is valued, and how participants engage. You may be acting as a facilitator, co-learner, co-designer, or connector. Each role creates different possibilities.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Map your system

Identify:

- who is involved
- what resources exist or are missing
- what constraints matter most
- what external factors influence success

### Step 2 — Define intended change

Be specific:

- What should participants do differently?
- What change do you hope the training contributes to?

### Step 3 — Build a simple Theory of Change

Link:

- what you do
- what participants gain
- what changes afterwards

Keep it realistic.

### Step 4 — Identify assumptions and risks

Ask:

- What must be true for this training to work?
- What is outside your control?
- What could prevent learners from applying what they learn?

### Step 5 — Reflect on your role

Decide:

- Are you delivering, facilitating, or co-creating?
- How much influence will participants have?
- What power dynamics matter here?

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> What level of change is realistic in your context?

- Individual skills only
- Changes in practice
- Institutional or community impact

Your answer determines how you design the training.

## Example

- **Context:** A team is designing climate data training for community organisers in a low-resource setting.
- **Decision:** Should they focus on advanced tools or practical low-tech approaches, and how much should participants shape the design?
- **Action:** They map the system and identify limited internet access, varied technical skills, and strong local knowledge. They decide to co-design parts of the training, use offline tools, and include locally relevant examples. They also shift their role from instructor to facilitator.
- **Outcome:** Participants are able to apply what they learn immediately, and the training can be adapted for future use in similar contexts.

# 2. Understanding Your Learners, Contexts, and Knowledge Systems

| Learning Outcomes | Guiding Questions |

## Why this matters

In the previous section, you mapped your training as an intervention within a broader system.

This section focuses on one of the most important parts of that system: your learners.

Training is most effective when it aligns with how people actually live, work, and learn. Well-designed training does not start with content. It starts with understanding:

- who your learners are
- what shapes their participation
- what knowledge they already bring

When you design with learners in mind, your training becomes more relevant, easier to apply, and more likely to lead to meaningful change.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What should I teach?”*  
    to:  
    *“Who are my learners, and what is possible in their context?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Learners, context, and prior knowledge"
    Effective training design depends on understanding who your learners are, what conditions shape their participation, and what knowledge they already bring.

**Primary and secondary audiences**  
Primary audiences are the direct participants in your training. Secondary audiences are the people they later support, train, or influence. If your participants act as intermediaries, your design must account for both immediate learning and downstream use.

**Needs assessment**  
A needs assessment is a practical way to understand what learners need to do, what they already know, and what constraints they face. It does not need to be complex. Short conversations, quick surveys, interviews, and co-mapping exercises are often enough.

**Context shapes learning**  
Access, time, language, culture, social expectations, and power dynamics are not side issues. They are design conditions.

**Lived experience and mental models**  
Learners bring prior knowledge, lived experience, habits, assumptions, and local expertise. New learning makes more sense when it connects to what already exists.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Identify your audiences

Clarify:

- who your primary participants are
- who the secondary audiences are
- what responsibilities participants carry beyond the training

### Step 2 — Understand learner realities

Gather information about:

- access to devices, internet, tools, and space
- time constraints and competing priorities
- language and communication preferences
- social and cultural context
- what success would look like from the learners’ perspective

### Step 3 — Conduct a simple needs assessment

Use:

- short conversations
- informal interviews
- quick surveys
- co-mapping exercises

Focus on:

- what learners already know
- what they need to do
- what challenges they face
- what strengths they already have

### Step 4 — Identify barriers and opportunities

Ask:

- What might prevent participation or success?
- What can you build on?
- What existing knowledge or practice can become part of the training?

### Step 5 — Design with learners, not only for them

Where possible:

- involve learners in shaping content
- use examples from their context
- validate different forms of knowledge
- avoid assuming that expert knowledge is the only knowledge that matters

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much do you need to adapt your training to fit your learners’ realities?

- Minimal adaptation
- Moderate adaptation
- Significant redesign

Your answer will shape your content, format, pace, and delivery choices.

## Example

- **Context:** A team is designing training for early-career researchers who will later train community members on climate data tools.
- **Decision:** How should the training account for both researchers and community members?
- **Action:** They conduct a simple needs assessment and learn that researchers are comfortable with technical tools, while community members have limited access and prefer practical examples. They design training that prepares researchers to adapt content, includes low-tech alternatives, and emphasises facilitation skills.
- **Outcome:** The training becomes more realistic, more transferable, and more useful for both the immediate and secondary audiences.

# 3. How People Learn: Cognitive, Social, and Contextual Perspectives

| Learning Outcomes | Guiding Questions |
|------------------|------------------|
| <ul><li>Explain key principles of how people learn (cognitive load, retrieval, spacing, social learning)</li><li>Identify factors that support or hinder effective learning</li><li>Apply strategies to reduce cognitive overload and improve retention</li><li>Design learning experiences that include retrieval, practice, and reinforcement</li><li>Incorporate social and contextual dimensions of learning into training design</li></ul> | <ul><li>How do people move information from short-term to long-term memory?</li><li>Where might cognitive overload occur in your training?</li><li>How will learners actively retrieve and apply knowledge?</li><li>How can learning be reinforced over time?</li><li>How will you incorporate social learning and collaboration?</li></ul> |

## Why this matters

In the previous section, you analysed your learners, their contexts, and the knowledge they bring.

This section focuses on how learning actually happens so that your design decisions support it.

Training is not effective simply because content is delivered. Learning happens when participants:

- engage actively
- connect new ideas to what they already know
- retrieve and apply knowledge
- revisit key ideas over time
- learn with and from others

If you design without understanding how learning works, you risk overwhelming learners, creating passive experiences, and limiting retention and real-world application.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“How do I present content?”*  
    to:  
    *“How do people actually learn—and how do I design for that?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "How learning strengthens"
    Learning becomes more durable when cognitive load is managed, learners actively retrieve and apply knowledge, key ideas are revisited over time, and interaction supports sense-making.

**Cognitive load**  
Working memory is limited. When too much new information appears at once, learners struggle to process it. Clear instructions, smaller chunks, and simpler materials reduce unnecessary load.

**Retrieval and application**  
Learning strengthens when people recall information, explain it, use it, and make decisions with it. Re-reading alone is rarely enough.

**Spacing and reinforcement**  
Single exposure is usually insufficient. Revisiting key ideas across sessions and in new contexts improves retention.

**Social learning**  
Discussion, collaboration, observation, and shared problem-solving help learners test understanding and refine ideas.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Reduce unnecessary cognitive load

- break content into smaller segments
- keep instructions clear and simple
- avoid introducing multiple new ideas at once
- remove avoidable complexity

### Step 2 — Design for active learning

For each part of your training, ask:

- What are learners doing?
- What will they produce, decide, explain, or apply?

Include:

- short exercises
- problem-solving tasks
- explanation and reflection activities

### Step 3 — Build in retrieval

Create opportunities where learners:

- recall key ideas
- answer questions without prompts
- apply concepts in realistic tasks

### Step 4 — Plan for reinforcement

- revisit key ideas across sessions
- use follow-up activities
- encourage continued practice beyond the session

### Step 5 — Use social learning intentionally

- include discussion prompts
- design small-group or peer activities
- create opportunities for learners to compare approaches and explain their thinking

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much active learning and retrieval will you build into your training?

- Mostly content delivery with limited activity
- A balanced mix of explanation and practice
- Practice-focused, highly interactive design

Your choice will directly affect retention, confidence, and transfer into practice.

## Example

- **Context:** A team is developing training on local climate risk assessment.
- **Decision:** Should they cover more content, or reduce content to create time for retrieval and practice?
- **Action:** They cut non-essential slides, break the material into smaller chunks, add short recall questions, revisit key ideas later in the session, and use pair discussion to test understanding.
- **Outcome:** Participants remember more, ask better questions, and are better able to apply the method afterwards.

## Key takeaways

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Learning is strengthened by retrieval, practice, reinforcement, and interaction, not only by explanation.

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Reducing unnecessary complexity creates more room for learners to think, apply, and remember.

# 4. Defining Impact, Outcomes, and Alignment

| Learning Outcomes | Guiding Questions |
|------------------|------------------|
| <ul><li>Distinguish between outputs, outcomes, and impact in training design</li><li>Define clear, observable, and actionable learning outcomes</li><li>Apply backward design to link outcomes, evidence, and activities</li><li>Diagnose and correct misalignment between outcomes, activities, and assessment</li><li>Identify meaningful indicators of success aligned with real-world application</li></ul> | <ul><li>What should learners be able to do as a result of this training?</li><li>How will you recognise whether learning has occurred?</li><li>Do your planned activities directly support your intended outcomes?</li><li>What evidence will demonstrate success in practice?</li><li>Where might misalignment exist between outcomes, activities, and assessment?</li></ul> |

## Why this matters

In the previous section, you explored how people learn and what supports effective learning.

This section focuses on making your training purposeful and aligned so that learning leads to meaningful outcomes.

Training often fails not because of poor delivery, but because:

- outcomes are unclear
- activities do not support what learners need to do
- success is not defined meaningfully

As a result, participants may engage with training but struggle to apply what they learned.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What should I include?”*  
    to:  
    *“What should learners be able to do—and how will I support and recognise that?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Outcomes and alignment"
    Effective training starts with clear outcomes, defines evidence of success, and then designs activities and assessment that support those outcomes.

**Outputs, outcomes, and impact**  
Outputs are immediate products of training, such as worksheets, discussions, or completed tasks. Outcomes are what learners can do afterwards. Impact is the longer-term change that results.

**Backward design**  
Backward design follows a simple sequence:

- Outcomes → Evidence → Activities

It keeps design focused on what matters.

**Constructive alignment**  
Alignment means your outcomes, activities, and assessment support each other. Learners should practise what they are expected to do, and assessment should reflect that same capability.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Define intended outcomes

Strong outcomes are:

- observable
- action-oriented
- relevant to real-world use

For example:

- “Interpret climate data to identify local risks”
- “Use a tool to analyse and communicate findings”

### Step 2 — Define evidence of success

Ask:

- What would demonstrate that learning has happened?
- What would participants produce, do, or decide?

Keep this manageable. Two or three clear indicators are enough.

### Step 3 — Design aligned activities

For each outcome, design activities where learners:

- practise the skill
- apply knowledge
- receive feedback

### Step 4 — Check alignment

Review your design:

- What is taught but not practised?
- What is practised but not assessed?
- Which activities do not contribute to outcomes?

### Step 5 — Ensure contextual relevance

Make sure outcomes reflect:

- what learners actually need to do
- the conditions they work within
- the decisions they are likely to face

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How specific and practice-oriented will your outcomes be?

- Broad and general
- Moderately specific
- Clear, observable, and linked to practice

Your choice will affect the quality of your activities, assessment, and evaluation.

## Example

- **Context:** A team wants to train participants on climate data interpretation.
- **Decision:** Should the main outcome be “understand climate data” or something more specific?
- **Action:** They rewrite the outcome as “interpret a local dataset to identify two practical risks and explain them to a non-specialist audience.” They then define evidence, design a matching activity, and remove unrelated content.
- **Outcome:** The training becomes clearer, more practical, and easier to assess.

# 5. Co-Designing Under Constraints

| Learning Outcomes | Guiding Questions |

## Why this matters

In the previous section, you defined outcomes and aligned your training design to support them.

This section focuses on how to design with people and within real-world constraints so that your training is relevant, usable, and sustainable.

Training is often designed by experts, for learners, without meaningful input from the people it aims to support. This can result in training that is difficult to apply, disconnected from real needs, or hard to sustain.

Most contexts also include constraints:

- limited time and resources
- uneven access to technology
- institutional and social dynamics
- competing priorities

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What would be ideal?”*  
    to:  
    *“What is possible, meaningful, and sustainable in this context?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Co-design under constraints"
    Co-design means involving participants or stakeholders in shaping training decisions, while constraints are the real conditions you must design within rather than ignore.

**Co-design**  
Participants can contribute to defining problems, shaping priorities, choosing examples, refining activities, and improving materials.

**Constraints**  
Time, access, infrastructure, language, institutional rules, social expectations, and power relations shape what is possible.

**Trade-offs and ethical participation**  
Design decisions often involve trade-offs between depth and breadth, participation and time, complexity and accessibility. Ethical co-design means participants have real influence and benefit from the process.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Identify who is shaping the training

Clarify:

- who is currently making key decisions
- who should be involved but is not yet included
- what decisions participants could meaningfully influence

### Step 2 — Identify key constraints

Map the constraints that matter most:

- time and scheduling
- technology and infrastructure
- materials and funding
- language and communication
- institutional or social dynamics

### Step 3 — Design with participants

Use manageable approaches such as:

- short pre-session consultations
- example gathering
- feedback on draft activities
- in-session adaptation based on participant input

### Step 4 — Make trade-offs explicit

Ask:

- What matters most for the outcomes?
- What is essential?
- What can be simplified, deferred, or removed?

### Step 5 — Ensure participation is meaningful

Check that participants:

- have real influence over something that matters
- are not only providing unpaid input with no visible effect
- see how their knowledge is used in the design

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much influence will participants have in shaping your training?

- Minimal input
- Moderate input
- Strong co-design

Your choice affects relevance, trust, and long-term usefulness.

## Example

- **Context:** A team is designing training for local organisations with very different levels of digital access.
- **Decision:** Should they finalise the training before speaking to participants, or involve them first?
- **Action:** They run short pre-session conversations, ask participants what examples are most relevant, and identify where offline options are needed. They cut some planned content to make room for locally relevant material.
- **Outcome:** The training is more realistic, more trusted, and easier for participants to use.

## Why this matters

In the previous section, you explored how to co-design training with participants and work within constraints.

This section focuses on turning your design into effective learning experiences where participants actively engage, contribute, and apply what they learn.

Training often breaks down at the activity level:

- activities are unclear
- participation is limited
- tasks do not support outcomes
- activities do not fit the context

Effective training depends on what learners do, not only on what you present.

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Structured, participatory activity design"
    Strong learning activities are clearly structured, actively engage learners, and provide enough support at the right time to move from guided participation to more independent practice.

**Activity structure**  
A well-structured activity includes:

- objective
- instructions
- materials
- time
- output
- feedback

Clarity reduces confusion and makes participation easier.

**Active learning**  
Active learning means learners are discussing, solving, analysing, making, or applying something meaningful.

**Scaffolding, sequencing, and psychological safety**  
Scaffolding provides support early and reduces it over time. Sequencing places simpler or more guided tasks before more complex ones. Psychological safety matters because learners participate more fully when they know they can contribute, try, and revise without embarrassment or unnecessary risk.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Design activities from outcomes

- what learners will do
- what they will produce or decide
- how this supports the outcome

### Step 2 — Structure each activity clearly

Specify:

- objective
- step-by-step instructions
- materials
- time
- output
- feedback

Write so that another facilitator could run the activity.

### Step 3 — Design for participation

Make participation intentional:

- use pair or small-group work
- allow think time before discussion
- offer multiple ways to contribute
- make expectations visible

### Step 4 — Apply scaffolding and sequencing

Support learners by:

- starting with guided examples
- increasing complexity gradually
- reducing support over time
- sequencing activities so each one prepares for the next

### Step 5 — Adapt for context and co-creation

Ensure activities:

- work with the available tools and time
- use locally relevant examples
- leave room for participant knowledge and examples
- support inclusive participation

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How structured and participatory will your learning activities be?

- Loosely structured and facilitator-led
- Moderately structured with some participation
- Highly structured, participant-driven, and interactive

Your choice affects clarity, engagement, and learning quality.

## Example

- **Context:** A team is designing a session to help researchers analyse and interpret climate data.
- **Decision:** Should they use a lecture-based approach or a structured hands-on activity?
- **Action:** They design an activity with a clear objective, step-by-step instructions, a real local dataset, small-group work, a defined output, and time for feedback. They begin with a guided example and then move to more independent work.
- **Outcome:** Participants actively engage, apply the method, and are more able to use it independently after the session.

## Why this matters

In the previous section, you designed learning activities that support participation and engagement.

This section focuses on how learning becomes usable and transferable through practice, feedback, and iteration.

Learning does not happen through exposure alone. Participants need:

- opportunities to practise
- feedback to improve
- time to revise and strengthen their work

Without this, learning remains theoretical and difficult to apply.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“Did I deliver the training?”*  
    to:  
    *“Did participants practise, receive feedback, and improve?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Practice, feedback, and iteration"
    Learning becomes more robust when learners apply skills in realistic tasks, receive useful feedback, and improve through repeated cycles of attempt, response, and revision.

**Practice**  
Practice should align with outcomes, reflect real use, and include enough support to help learners succeed.

**Feedback**  
Useful feedback is specific, timely, and actionable. It helps learners understand what is working and what to improve next.

**Iteration and productive challenge**  
Improvement happens through cycles of attempt, feedback, and revision. Learners do not need to get everything right on the first try. Productive challenge is part of learning when it is supported and followed by feedback.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Design meaningful practice

Ensure learners:

- apply key skills
- work on realistic tasks
- connect practice directly to outcomes

Use a mix of:

- guided practice
- independent practice
- collaborative practice

### Step 2 — Integrate feedback into activities

Plan feedback as part of the activity:

- include checkpoints
- define what good performance looks like
- allow time for peer and facilitator feedback

### Step 3 — Make feedback useful

Feedback should:

- focus on specific aspects of performance
- include next steps
- arrive while revision is still possible

### Step 4 — Build in iteration and versioning

Create opportunities for learners to revise and improve.

Simple approaches include:

- first attempt → feedback → revised version
- returning to a task in a later session
- using simple versions such as v0, v1, and v2 to make progress visible

### Step 5 — Enable peer learning

Support participants to learn from each other:

- use peer review
- encourage explanation of approaches
- make feedback respectful and constructive
- ensure participation remains inclusive

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much opportunity will learners have to practise, receive feedback, and improve?

- Limited practice with minimal feedback
- Practice with some feedback but little revision
- Structured practice with feedback and multiple opportunities to improve

Your choice affects how well learning translates into real-world capability.

## Example

- **Context:** A team is designing training for researchers to analyse and communicate climate data.
- **Decision:** Should participants complete a single activity, or have opportunities to improve their work?
- **Action:** They design a sequence where participants complete an initial analysis, receive peer and facilitator feedback, and then revise their work. They label the versions v0, v1, and v2 so progress is visible. They also include a follow-up discussion on what changed and why.
- **Outcome:** Participants produce stronger analyses, understand their mistakes, and gain confidence in applying the method independently.

# 8. Assessment and Evaluation for Learning and Impact

| Learning Outcomes | Guiding Questions |
|------------------|------------------|
| <ul><li>Distinguish between assessment (learning-focused) and evaluation (impact-focused)</li><li>Design formative and summative assessments aligned with learning outcomes</li><li>Define clear, observable indicators of success</li><li>Select appropriate methods for collecting evidence of learning and impact</li><li>Evaluate the usefulness of data by distinguishing signal from noise</li></ul> | <ul><li>How will you know if learning has occurred?</li><li>What is the difference between assessing learning and evaluating impact?</li><li>Are your assessments aligned with your intended outcomes?</li><li>What indicators best reflect meaningful success?</li><li>What evidence will you collect, and how will you use it?</li></ul> |

## Why this matters

In the previous section, you designed opportunities for practice, feedback, and iteration.

This section focuses on how to recognise learning and understand impact so that your training improves over time.

Training is often evaluated using attendance or satisfaction. These can be useful, but they do not tell you:

- whether learning happened
- whether participants can apply what they learned
- whether the training made a meaningful difference

Effective training needs assessment to understand learning and evaluation to understand impact.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“Did the training run?”*  
    to:  
    *“Did learning happen, and did it lead to meaningful change?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Assessment and evaluation"
    Assessment focuses on what learners can do, while evaluation focuses on whether the training contributed to the intended outcomes or broader change.

**Assessment**  
Assessment looks at what learners know, produce, decide, or do. It should align with the outcomes you defined earlier.

**Evaluation**  
Evaluation looks beyond the learning activity itself. It asks whether the training led to useful change in practice, confidence, behaviour, or other intended outcomes.

**Indicators, evidence, and signal**  
Indicators are observable signs of learning or change. Evidence is what you collect to judge those indicators. Useful evidence gives signal, not just noise.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Define what success looks like

Start with your outcomes and ask:

- What would learners be able to do if learning is successful?
- What would meaningful change look like afterwards?

Define two or three clear indicators.

### Step 2 — Design aligned assessment

For each outcome, decide how learners will demonstrate it.

Examples include:

- completing a task
- producing an output
- making and justifying a decision
- applying a method in context

### Step 3 — Use formative and summative assessment

Include:

- formative assessment during learning to support improvement
- summative assessment at the end to judge achievement

Use formative approaches wherever possible.

### Step 4 — Collect useful and feasible evidence

Use methods such as:

- observation
- outputs or artefacts
- short reflections
- brief follow-up questions
- peer or facilitator review

Choose methods you can realistically use.

### Step 5 — Focus on signal, not noise

Useful signal might include learners successfully applying a method or producing a better-quality output. Noise might include attendance or positive comments alone.

### Step 6 — Use evaluation to improve

Use what you learn to:

- refine activities
- identify gaps
- improve future delivery
- decide what to keep, change, or remove

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much effort will you invest in meaningful assessment and evaluation?

- Basic attendance and satisfaction only
- Some assessment of learning
- Clear indicators, aligned assessment, and useful evidence

Your choice affects how well you can understand learning and improve the training.

## Example

- **Context:** A team has run a climate data training session.
- **Decision:** Should they judge success by attendance and participant satisfaction alone?
- **Action:** They define indicators linked to the outcomes, such as whether participants can interpret a dataset and explain two practical implications. They assess this through a task, observe how learners perform, and ask a short follow-up question about application after the session.
- **Outcome:** They get clearer evidence of learning and a better basis for improving the training.

## Why this matters

In the previous sections, you designed training as an intervention, understood your learners and context, defined outcomes, created activities, built in practice and feedback, and planned for assessment and evaluation.

This section focuses on what happens next:

- Can your training be used again by you or someone else without starting from scratch?
- Do you need to create new materials at all, or could you reuse or adapt what already exists?

This is where Open Educational Resources become relevant.

Designing for reuse is not only generous. It is also practical. Reusable materials help your future self, colleagues, and collaborators work more efficiently.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“Designing training for a single delivery”*  
    to:  
    *“Designing training that you can reuse, adapt, and share.”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Reuse, adapt, create"
    Training materials can be reused as they are, adapted for a different context, or created from scratch when needed. Strong design usually combines all three approaches intentionally.

**Reusable materials**  
Reusable materials are clear, structured, and understandable without depending on your memory or explanation.

**Hidden assumptions**  
Materials often contain implicit expectations about prior knowledge, tools, language, examples, or context. These assumptions reduce reusability unless they are made visible.

**Why openness matters**  
Openness becomes useful when materials can be accessed, understood, adapted, and shared. This section introduces that shift. The details of openness, licensing, and publishing come in Module 2.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Assess your current materials

Ask:

- Can I run this training again without redesigning it?
- What would I struggle to remember or explain?
- What depends too much on me being present?

### Step 2 — Decide whether to reuse, adapt, or create

Before creating new materials, ask:

- Does something already exist that I could use?
- Can I adapt it effectively?
- Would adaptation take more effort than creating something focused and simple?

Reuse is not always easier. Searching, checking quality, and adapting can also take time. The best choice depends on fit, effort, and value.

### Step 3 — Make materials more reusable

Improve materials by:

- clarifying instructions
- making activities self-contained
- documenting required materials and outputs
- separating core content from local examples where useful

### Step 4 — Identify and remove hidden assumptions

Ask:

- What prior knowledge is assumed?
- What tools or resources are required?
- What is obvious to me but not written down?

Make these visible.

### Step 5 — Design for future use

Think about:

- how your future self might reuse this
- what another facilitator would need
- what would need to change in a different context

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How will you approach developing your training materials?

- Create everything from scratch
- Reuse or adapt where possible
- Combine reuse, adaptation, and creation intentionally

Your choice affects time, effort, clarity, and long-term usefulness.

## Example

- **Context:** A facilitator has delivered a strong workshop once and now wants to use it again with a different audience.
- **Decision:** Should they simply repeat the session, redesign everything, or improve what already exists?
- **Action:** They review the materials and notice that several instructions depend on memory, some examples are too context-specific, and one useful external resource could replace a section they planned to rebuild. They clarify instructions, document assumptions, and decide to adapt one existing resource instead of creating new material from scratch.
- **Outcome:** The training becomes easier to reuse, easier to adapt, and more efficient to maintain over time.
