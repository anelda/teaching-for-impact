## Why this matters

Creating training materials from scratch takes time.

In many cases:

- similar materials already exist  
- others have solved similar problems  
- useful explanations, examples, or activities are available  

If you don’t check what exists, you may end up:

- duplicating effort  
- spending time where it’s not needed  
- missing approaches that are already working well  

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."
    *“I need to create something for this.”*  

    to:

    *“What already exists, and where should I invest my effort for the greatest impact?”*

    👉 Good design is not about starting from scratch—it is about making deliberate decisions about where your time and effort matter most.

---

## Core concept

In the previous section, you were introduced to a key decision:

> Should you reuse, adapt, or create new materials?

!!! abstract "Reuse"
    Using an existing resource as-is.

!!! abstract "Adaptation"
    Changing a resource so it better fits your learners, context, or goals.

!!! abstract "Creation"
    Developing new materials when suitable options do not exist.

This section helps you apply that decision in practice.

Reusing Open Educational Resources (OER) involves:

- finding materials that already exist  
- evaluating whether they are useful  
- adapting them where needed  
- integrating them into your training  

---

### Important clarification

This is not about always reusing.

Reuse is not always faster. Finding and adapting materials can take time.

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    The goal is not to reuse by default—it is to choose the option that gives you the best result for the least effort.

---

## Practical guidance

### 1. Start by checking what already exists

Before creating new material, pause and ask:

- Does something like this already exist?  
- Has someone solved this problem before?  

Even partial matches can save time and effort.

---

### 2. Where to find OER

Start with a few reliable sources rather than searching everywhere.

Look for:

- university repositories  
- open course materials  
- training toolkits  
- NGO or community resources  
- subject-specific platforms  

Also consider:

- materials shared by colleagues  
- resources used in similar programmes  

---

### Using AI tools to support reuse and adaptation

AI tools (such as large language models) can help you:

- find relevant resources more quickly  
- summarise and compare materials  
- generate drafts when nothing suitable exists  

---

#### How to use them effectively

⚙️ **In practice**

Use AI tools to:

- explore options  
- generate starting points  
- support adaptation  

But your role remains critical:

- assessing relevance  
- ensuring accuracy  
- deciding what fits your context  

---

#### Risks and ethical considerations

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Assuming AI-generated content is accurate or open to reuse.

AI-generated outputs may:

- include content without clear attribution  
- contain incorrect or misleading information  
- generate false or fabricated references  

---

#### Practical approach

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Treat AI outputs as drafts—not final materials.

When using AI tools:

- treat outputs as drafts, not final materials  
- verify key facts and references  
- adapt content to your context  
- avoid assuming AI-generated content is automatically open  

---

### 3. Evaluate relevance and quality

Evaluating resources is not always straightforward.

You are often making trade-offs between:

- quality and relevance  
- completeness and adaptability  
- effort to adapt vs effort to create  

---

### What to consider

When reviewing a resource, ask:

- Does this support the learning outcome?  
- Will my learners understand and relate to it?  
- Is it accurate and clear?  

---


!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    You do not need a perfect resource—only one that is *good enough* to support your outcomes.

---

### 4. Assess adaptability for your context

A resource may be high quality but still not usable as-is.

Check whether it can be:

- edited or modified  
- simplified or expanded  
- adapted to local examples or language  
- used with available tools  

---

!!! abstract "Localisation*"  
    Adapting a resource so it fits a specific cultural, linguistic, or contextual setting.

---

### 5. Decide: reuse, adapt, or create

This is a key decision point.

You are balancing:

- how well the resource fits  
- how much effort adaptation will take  
- how much time you have  

---

⚙️ **In practice — decision rule**

- **Reuse** → if the resource fits well with minimal changes  
- **Adapt** → if it is close and can be improved with reasonable effort  
- **Create** → if adapting would take more effort than starting fresh  

---

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Choose the option that gives you the best result for the least effort. In practice, you may combine these approaches.

---

### 6. Integrate into your design

Do not treat reused materials as separate.

Ensure they:

- align with your outcomes  
- fit into your activities  
- match your learners’ context  
- can be understood without relying on your explanation.

---

!!! warning "Common mistakes"

    - Using the first resource you find without evaluating it  
    - Choosing convenience over relevance  
    - Underestimating the effort required to adapt materials  
    - Using materials that do not fit your context

## Optional deeper insight

Reuse is part of a broader shift toward shared knowledge.

Instead of working in isolation:

- knowledge is built collectively  
- resources improve over time  

Good reuse decisions:

- save effort  
- improve quality  
- increase impact
