## Why this matters

In the previous section, you identified resources that could support your training.

But before using them, it is important to understand:

> Are you actually allowed to use, adapt, and share these materials?

Not all freely available materials are open.

Some resources are:

- easy to access  
- free to download  

…but still:

- cannot be modified  
- cannot be shared  
- cannot be reused in your own materials  

If you don’t check this, you may:

- invest time adapting something you are not allowed to change  
- create materials you cannot share  
- unintentionally misuse someone else’s work  

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“This is available, so I can use it.”*  

    to:

    *“I understand what I am allowed to do—and can design accordingly.”*

    👉 Openness is not about access—it is about permissions that determine whether you can reuse, adapt, and share.

---

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Open Educational Resources (OER)"
    Teaching and learning materials that can be reused, adapted, and shared because they have clear, open permissions.

!!! abstract "License"
    A set of rules that defines what you are allowed to do with a resource.

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    “Open” does not mean “free.”  
    It means you have permission to use, adapt, and share.

---

### The 5 key permissions (5Rs)

Open resources are often described in terms of five permissions:

- **Retain** — you can keep a copy  
- **Reuse** — you can use the material in your training  
- **Revise** — you can modify or adapt it  
- **Remix** — you can combine it with other materials  
- **Redistribute** — you can share it with others  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    If you cannot adapt or share a resource, it is not truly open for your purposes.

---

### Why this matters in practice

If these permissions are restricted, it affects your choices.

For example:

- If you cannot modify a resource, you cannot adapt it to your context  
- If you cannot share it, your training cannot be reused by others  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Assuming that anything online can be reused.

---

## Practical guidance

### 1. Check permissions before you invest time

Before adapting or integrating a resource, check:

- Is there a license?  
- What does it allow you to do?  

---

⚙️ **In practice**

If there is:

- **No license** → assume you cannot reuse or adapt it  
- **An unclear license** → proceed with caution  

---

!!! tip Key takeaway"
    Always check permissions before you invest effort in adapting a resource.

---

### 2. Understand common open licenses (simple view)

Many OER use **Creative Commons (CC)** licenses.

You do not need to memorise them—focus on what they allow.

---

!!! abstract "Creative Commons licenses"
    Standardised licenses that clearly state how a resource can be used, adapted, and shared.

---

- **Creative Commons Attribution (CC BY)**  
  → You can reuse and adapt, as long as you give credit  

- **Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike (CC BY-SA)**  
  → You can adapt, but must share your version under the same license  

- **Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial (CC BY-NC)**  
  → You can reuse and adapt, but not for commercial use  

- **Creative Commons Attribution-NoDerivatives (CC BY-ND)**  
  → You can reuse, but cannot modify  

---

### What this means for your decisions

⚙️ **In practice — quick rules**

- Want to **adapt materials** → avoid **ND (NoDerivatives)**  
- Want to **share your materials widely** → be cautious with **NC (NonCommercial)**  
- Want **maximum flexibility** → use **CC BY**

---

!!!tip "Key takeaway"
    Your ability to adapt and share depends on the license—not on where you found the resource.

---

### 3. Provide attribution

Most open licenses require attribution.

---

!!! abstract "Attribution"
    Giving credit to the original creator of a resource.

---

This usually includes:

- the author  
- the title  
- the source  
- the license  

---

⚙️ **In practice**

If you are unsure:

> include attribution—it is rarely wrong to do so.

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    If you use someone else’s work, always give credit.

---

### 4. Be careful when combining resources

If you remix materials, check that:

- licenses are compatible  
- you are allowed to combine them  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"  
    Combining resources without checking license compatibility.

---

### Example

You may not be able to combine:

- a resource that cannot be modified  
- with one that requires open sharing  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Not all open resources can be combined freely.

---

### 5. When using AI-generated content

AI tools can complicate licensing.

Outputs may:

- draw on existing materials  
- lack clear attribution  
- include copyrighted content  

---

!!! warning  "Common mistake"
    Assuming AI-generated content is automatically open.

---

⚙️ **In practice**

When using AI:

- do not assume content is open  
- verify sources where possible  
- treat outputs as drafts  
- ensure you are comfortable sharing what you produce  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"  
    Do not assume AI-generated content is safe to reuse or share without checking.

---

!!! warning "Common mistakes"

  - Assuming “free” means “open”  
  - Ignoring license terms  
  - Using materials that cannot be adapted  
  - Not providing attribution  
  - Combining incompatible resources

## Optional deeper insight

Openness is defined by permissions, not access.

Clear licensing enables:

- reuse  
- adaptation  
- sharing  

Without it, reuse becomes difficult or restricted.
