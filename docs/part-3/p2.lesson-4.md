## Why this matters

In Module 1, you designed training that works **with you as the facilitator**.

In the bridging section and earlier Module 2 sections, you explored how to reuse and adapt resources.

This section focuses on a critical shift:

> Can your materials work without you—and in contexts different from your own?

If your materials depend on:

- your explanations  
- your memory  
- your specific context  

then:

- others cannot use them effectively  
- future you may struggle to reuse them  
- adaptation becomes difficult :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“This works when I teach it.”*  
    to:  
    *“This works without me—and can be used by others.”*

## Core concepts

### Accessibility and usability

!!! abstract "Accessibility and usability"
    Accessibility ensures materials can be obtained and opened, while usability ensures they can be understood and used without additional explanation.

- **Accessible** → others can access and open the materials  
- **Usable** → others can understand and use them independently  

Design implication: materials must be both accessible and usable to support reuse.

---

### Independence of use

Materials should not rely on:

- facilitator explanation  
- unstated assumptions  
- contextual knowledge  

Design implication: design for independent use by default.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Make materials usable without your presence

Ensure materials include:

- clear, step-by-step instructions  
- defined outputs or expected results  

Strengthen usability by:

- adding examples  
- clarifying common points of confusion  
- anticipating likely questions  

---

### Step 2 — Ensure materials can be accessed

Check that materials:

- open on common devices  
- are available in accessible formats  

Where possible:

- provide downloadable versions  
- reduce file size requirements  
- enable offline access  

---

### Step 3 — Use editable and adaptable formats

Support reuse by:

- using formats that can be edited  
- structuring content into sections  

Where possible:

- design materials so parts can be reused independently  

---

### Step 4 — Make assumptions visible

Identify and state:

- required prior knowledge  
- required tools or resources  
- expected level of familiarity  

Also:

- indicate where adaptation may be needed  

---

### Step 5 — Improve clarity and consistency

Ensure materials:

- use simple, clear language  
- explain key terms where needed  
- follow a consistent structure  

Where possible:

- standardise terminology  
- improve flow across activities  

---

### Step 6 — Test for usability

Review materials from another perspective:

- read them as if you are not the creator  
- identify unclear instructions or gaps  

Where possible:

- ask someone else to review  
- ask someone to use the material without your help  

---

### Step 7 — Design for transfer across contexts

Support reuse by:

- avoiding unnecessary context-specific detail  
- separating core content from examples  

Where possible:

- indicate what can be changed  
- highlight where adaptation is expected  

---

### Step 8 — Balance specificity and flexibility

Not all materials should be generic.

Maintain:

- meaningful examples  
- relevant context  

While ensuring:

- structure can be reused  
- examples can be adapted  

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How usable and accessible will your materials be beyond your own delivery?

- Minimal (works mainly with you as facilitator)  
- Improved (some independent use possible)  
- Fully usable (can be used and adapted without your presence)  

Your choice affects reuse, scalability, and long-term value.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team has developed a training activity on climate data analysis.

**Decision:**  
Can others use this activity without the original facilitator?

**Action:**  
They:

- clarify instructions  
- define expected outputs  
- replace implicit assumptions with explicit guidance  
- provide editable materials  
- ask a colleague to test the activity independently  

**Outcome:**  
The activity can be used by others, adapted for new contexts, and reused in future training.
