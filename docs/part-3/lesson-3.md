## Why this matters

In the previous sections, you identified and evaluated resources you could use.

However, most resources are not designed for your specific context.

They may:

- use unfamiliar examples  
- assume access to tools you do not have  
- be written for a different audience  
- reflect different cultural or institutional settings  

If you use materials as they are:

- learners may struggle to relate  
- activities may not work as intended  
- learning may not transfer into practice  

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

      *“This resource is good—I’ll use it as it is.”*  

      to:

      *“How do I change this so it actually works for my learners and context?”*

      👉 Effective training is not about finding the perfect resource—it is about reshaping materials so they are relevant, usable, and aligned with your goals.

---

## Core concept

Using OER is not just about finding materials—it is about making them work for your learners and your setting.

This typically involves three related practices:

---

### Adaptation

Changing the *structure or content* of a resource so that it better supports your learning goals.

You might:
- simplify or expand explanations  
- change the sequence of activities  
- modify instructions  
- adjust the level of difficulty  

👉 Focus: *Does this help learners achieve the outcome?*

---

### Localisation

**Localising** means changing the *context* of a resource so that it is meaningful and relevant to your learners.

You might:
- replace examples with local ones  
- adjust language or terminology  
- reflect local realities and constraints  
- change references to tools or systems  

👉 Focus: *Does this make sense in this context?*

---

### Remixing

**Remixing** means combining parts of multiple resources into a new, coherent whole.

You might:
- combine activities from different sources  
- integrate explanations and examples  
- restructure content into a new format  

👉 Focus: *Do these pieces work together as one experience?*

---

### How they relate

In practice, you will often combine all three:

- adapt structure  
- localise context  
- remix resources  

📌 **Key takeaway**  
Effective training is rarely built from a single source — it is usually assembled and refined.

---

## Practical guidance

### 1. Start with your outcomes

Before making changes, return to your learning outcomes.

Ask:

> What do learners need to be able to do?

This helps you decide what to:
- keep  
- change  
- remove  

📌 **Key takeaway**  
Adaptation decisions should be driven by outcomes, not convenience.

---

### 2. Focus your effort where it matters most

You do not need to change everything.

The goal is to focus your effort where it will have the biggest impact.

---

### How to prioritise

Start with:

1. What affects whether learners understand the material  
2. What affects whether the activity works in your context  

---

⚠️ **Common mistake**  
Changing too much without improving learning outcomes.

---

### Then decide what to change

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- adjust instructions so they are clear  
- replace at least one example with something relevant  

---

**If you have time:**
- simplify language  
- adjust the level of detail  
- reorder steps or activities  

---

**Strong practice:**
- redesign activities to better fit your context  
- align content more closely with outcomes  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
If learners do not recognise the context, learning is harder to apply.

---

### 3. Localise for relevance

Context matters for learning.

Even small changes can make a big difference.

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- replace unfamiliar examples  

---

**If you have time:**
- adjust terminology and language  
- reflect local challenges or use cases  

---

**Strong practice:**
- redesign examples and activities around local realities  

---

### Key question

> Would learners recognise this in their own context?

---

### 4. Remix carefully

Remixing involves combining different materials into one experience.

This requires judgement.

---

### What to look for

When combining resources, check:

- Do they support the same outcome?  
- Are they at a similar level of complexity?  
- Do they use consistent language and concepts?  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
Combining good materials poorly reduces clarity.

---

### Key idea

> Good materials combined without integration can reduce clarity rather than improve it.

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- ensure all parts support the same outcome  

---

**If you have time:**
- align level and complexity  
- remove duplication  

---

**Strong practice:**
- restructure materials into a coherent learning experience  

---

### Common issue

Good materials combined poorly can lead to:
- confusion  
- inconsistency  
- overload  

---

### 5. Maintain coherence

After adapting or remixing, step back and review. Check that someone else could understand how to use this without additional explanation.

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- check that instructions make sense  
- ensure activities align with outcomes  

---

**If you have time:**
- improve flow between activities  
- ensure consistent terminology  

---

**Strong practice:**
- refine the full learning experience  

---

⚠️ **Common mistake**  
Assuming materials make sense because you created them.

---

### 6. Keep track of changes

Documenting changes helps you—and others—reuse your materials later.

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- note what you changed  

---

**If you have time:**
- note why you changed it  

---

**Strong practice:**
- document how others could adapt it further  

---

## Common mistakes

- Using materials without adapting them  
- Changing too much without a clear purpose  
- Mixing resources without integrating them  
- Keeping examples that are not relevant  
- Ignoring local constraints

Learning is shaped by context.

Adaptation and localisation:
- improve relevance  
- support engagement  
- enable transfer into practice  

No resource is universally applicable—adaptation is a normal part of effective design.
