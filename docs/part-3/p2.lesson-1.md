## Why this matters

In the previous section, you considered how to design your materials so they can be reused and adapted.

This section focuses on the next step:

> How do you find and evaluate materials that already exist—so you do not start from scratch unnecessarily?

In many cases:

- similar materials already exist  
- others have solved similar problems  
- useful explanations, examples, or activities are available  

If you do not check what exists, you may:

- duplicate effort  
- spend time where it is not needed  
- miss approaches that are already effective  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“I need to create something for this.”*  
    to:  
    *“What already exists, and where should I invest my effort?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Reuse, adapt, create

!!! abstract "Reuse, adapt, create"
    Three approaches to working with existing resources: using them as-is, modifying them, or creating new materials when needed.

- **Reuse** — use a resource without changes  
- **Adapt** — modify it to fit your context  
- **Create** — develop new material when necessary  

Design implication: effective design often combines these approaches.

---

### Evaluating resources

!!! abstract "Evaluating resources"
    Assessing whether a resource is relevant, usable, and worth adapting.

Evaluation involves considering:

- alignment with outcomes  
- clarity and quality  
- fit with your learners and context  

Design implication: you are not looking for perfect resources—only those that are useful.

---

### Adaptability

A resource may be useful but not directly usable.

Adaptability depends on whether it can be:

- edited or modified  
- simplified or expanded  
- adjusted to local context  

Design implication: choose resources that can realistically be adapted.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Start by checking what exists

Before creating new materials, ask:

- Has someone already created something similar?  
- Can I use part of an existing resource?  

Even partial matches can reduce effort.

---

### Step 2 — Search strategically

Focus on a few reliable sources:

- university repositories  
- open course materials  
- training toolkits  
- NGO or community resources  

Also consider:

- materials shared by colleagues  
- resources used in similar programmes  

Avoid searching too broadly—prioritise quality over quantity.

---

### Step 3 — Evaluate quickly

When reviewing a resource, ask:

- Does this support my learning outcomes?  
- Will my learners understand and relate to it?  
- Is it clear and accurate?  

You are making a practical decision, not a perfect one.

---

### Step 4 — Assess adaptability

Check whether the resource can be adapted:

- Can you edit or modify it?  
- Can you replace examples with local ones?  
- Can it work within your constraints?  

If adaptation is difficult, it may not be worth using.

---

### Step 5 — Decide: reuse, adapt, or create

Use a simple rule:

- **Reuse** → if it fits with minimal changes  
- **Adapt** → if it is close and can be improved  
- **Create** → if adapting would take more effort than starting fresh  

In practice, you will often combine these approaches.

---

### Step 6 — Integrate into your design

Ensure the resource:

- aligns with your outcomes  
- fits into your activities  
- works for your learners and context  
- can be used without relying on your explanation  

Do not treat reused materials as separate—integrate them fully.

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> Where will you invest your effort in developing materials?

- Create everything from scratch  
- Reuse or adapt where possible  
- Combine reuse, adaptation, and creation intentionally  

Your choice affects time, effort, and quality.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing training on climate data analysis for early-career researchers.

**Decision:**  
Should they create new materials or use existing ones?

**Action:**  
They:

- search for existing resources on data interpretation  
- select one activity that aligns with their outcomes  
- adapt examples to local contexts  
- integrate the activity into their training design  

They create new material only where no suitable resources exist.

**Outcome:**  
They reduce development time, improve quality, and focus effort where it matters most.

## Key takeaways

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Do not start from scratch by default—check what already exists first.
