## Why this matters

In the previous section, you identified and evaluated resources that could support your training.

This section focuses on a critical question:

> Are you actually allowed to use, adapt, and share these materials?

Not all resources that are easy to access are open.

Some materials may be:

- free to access  
- easy to download  

…but still:

- cannot be modified  
- cannot be shared  
- cannot be reused in your own materials  

If you do not check this, you may:

- invest time adapting something you are not allowed to change  
- create materials you cannot legally share  
- unintentionally misuse someone else’s work :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“This is available, so I can use it.”*  
    to:  
    *“I understand what I am allowed to do—and can design accordingly.”*

## Core concepts

### Open educational resources

!!! abstract "Open educational resources (OER)"
    Teaching and learning materials that can be reused, adapted, and shared because they have clear permissions.

Openness is defined by what you are allowed to do—not by whether something is free or accessible.

---

### Licenses

!!! abstract "License"
    A set of permissions that defines how a resource can be used, adapted, and shared.

A license tells you:

- whether you can modify a resource  
- whether you can share it  
- under what conditions you can use it  

Design implication: always check permissions before investing effort.

---

### The 5 permissions (5Rs)

Open resources are often described through five key permissions:

- **Retain** — keep a copy  
- **Reuse** — use in your training  
- **Revise** — modify or adapt  
- **Remix** — combine with other materials  
- **Redistribute** — share with others  

Design implication: if you cannot adapt or share a resource, it may not meet your needs.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Check permissions first

Before using or adapting a resource, check:

- Is there a license?  
- What does it allow you to do?  

If there is:

- **no license** → assume you cannot reuse or adapt  
- **an unclear license** → proceed cautiously  

---

### Step 2 — Understand common licenses (practical view)

Many open resources use Creative Commons (CC) licenses.

Focus on what they allow:

- **CC BY** → reuse and adapt with attribution  
- **CC BY-SA** → adapt and share under the same license  
- **CC BY-NC** → reuse and adapt, but not for commercial use  
- **CC BY-ND** → reuse only, no modification  

Use simple rules:

- want to **adapt** → avoid ND  
- want to **share widely** → be cautious with NC  
- want **maximum flexibility** → prefer CC BY  

---

### Step 3 — Provide attribution

Most open licenses require attribution.

This typically includes:

- the author  
- the title  
- the source  
- the license  

If unsure:

- include attribution  

---

### Step 4 — Check compatibility when combining resources

When combining materials:

- ensure licenses allow modification  
- ensure they can be combined legally  

Some combinations may not be allowed.

---

### Step 5 — Be cautious with AI-generated content

AI-generated content may:

- include material derived from existing sources  
- lack clear attribution  
- have unclear permissions  

When using AI:

- do not assume outputs are open  
- verify where possible  
- treat outputs as drafts  

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How carefully will you consider permissions when selecting resources?

- Minimal checking (risk of misuse or limitations)  
- Basic checking (some awareness of permissions)  
- Intentional checking (clear understanding of what is allowed)  

Your choice affects whether your materials can be adapted, reused, and shared.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team selects an online resource for a training activity.

**Decision:**  
Can they adapt and include it in their materials?

**Action:**  
They:

- check the license  
- confirm that adaptation is allowed  
- provide attribution  
- adapt examples to fit their context  

They avoid using a second resource that cannot be modified.

**Outcome:**  
They use materials they are allowed to adapt and share, avoiding future limitations.
