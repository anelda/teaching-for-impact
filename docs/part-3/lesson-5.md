## Why this matters

You have now:

- identified and evaluated existing resources  
- adapted and localised materials for your context  
- improved accessibility and usability  

The next step is to consider:

> How will others (and your future self) find, use, and benefit from your materials?

If your materials are not shared clearly and sustainably:

- they may be difficult to find  
- others may not know how to use them  
- they may not be reused or improved  

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."
      *“I’ve created something useful.”*  

      to:

      *“Others can find, understand, use, and build on this.”*

      👉 Sharing is not just about making materials available—it can go much further if it enables reuse, adaptation, and long-term value.

---

## Core concept

Publishing OER is not just about uploading files.

It involves:

- making materials **findable**  
- making permissions **clear**  
- providing enough context for others to **use and adapt them**  
- ensuring they can be **maintained over time**  

---

🔎 **Publishing**  
Making your materials available so others can find and access them.

🔎 **Licensing**  
Defining what others are allowed to do with your materials.

🔎 **Attribution**  
Giving credit to original creators (including yourself, when reused).

🔎 **Versioning**  
Keeping track of changes to materials over time (e.g. v1, v2).

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
If others cannot find, understand, or reuse your materials, sharing them has limited impact.

---

### Key question

> Could someone find, understand, and use your materials without relying on your explanation, memory, or original context?

## Practical guidance

### 1. Decide what to share

You do not need to share everything.

Focus on materials that:
- are complete enough to be useful  
- can be understood without additional explanation  
- have value beyond your immediate context  

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- select one activity or resource to share  

---

**If you have time:**
- share a small set of related materials  

---

**Strong practice:**
- share a complete, structured training package  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
Start small—sharing something usable is better than sharing nothing.

---

### 2. Choose where to publish

Your materials should be easy to find and access.

Common options include:
- institutional repositories  
- open platforms (e.g. Zenodo, Figshare)  
- project or organisational websites  
- shared folders (with clear structure)  

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- store materials in a stable, accessible location  

---

**If you have time:**
- choose a platform that allows sharing and reuse  

---

**Strong practice:**
- publish in a repository with persistent links (e.g. DOI)  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
If people cannot reliably find your materials, they cannot reuse them.

---

### 3. Make permissions clear

Others need to know what they are allowed to do with your materials.

This requires making an intentional decision about licensing.

---

### Step 1: Choose a license

Select a license and include it clearly with your materials.

If you are unsure, a commonly used and flexible option is:

- **Creative Commons Attribution (CC BY)**

This allows others to:
- reuse  
- adapt  
- share  

as long as they give credit.

---

### Step 2: Consider your intent

Before finalising your choice, ask:

- Do I want others to adapt this?  
- Do I want to restrict commercial use?  
- Do I want adaptations to be shared openly?  

Your answers will influence your license choice.

---

### Step 3: Refine your choice

Based on your intent:

- If you want maximum reuse → use **CC BY**  
- If you want adaptations shared openly → use **CC BY-SA**  
- If you want to restrict commercial use → consider **CC BY-NC**  
- If you do not want modification → use **CC BY-ND** (note: this limits reuse significantly)  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
A license is not just a label—it determines how your work can be used.

---

⚠️ **Common mistake**  
Choosing a license without thinking about how you want your materials to be used.

---

### 4. Provide context for use

Your materials should not depend on your explanation.

Provide enough information so others can understand:

- what the material is for  
- who it is for  
- how it is used  

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- include a short description of the resource  

---

**If you have time:**
- add instructions for use  
- describe intended audience  

---

**Strong practice:**
- include facilitator notes  
- explain how the material can be adapted  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
Materials without context are difficult to reuse.

---

### 5. Include attribution and sources

If you reused or adapted materials:

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- credit original sources  

---

**If you have time:**
- indicate what you changed  

---

**Strong practice:**
- provide clear attribution and adaptation notes  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
Good attribution supports reuse and builds trust.

---

### 6. Make your materials easy to use and adapt

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- share editable versions  

---

**If you have time:**
- organise files clearly  

---

**Strong practice:**
- structure materials so parts can be reused independently  

---

⚠️ **Common mistake**  
Sharing materials in formats that are difficult to edit or reuse.

---

### 7. Plan for sustainability

Your materials may change over time.

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- keep a version you can update  

---

**If you have time:**
- note version or date  

---

**Strong practice:**
- update materials based on feedback and reuse  

---

### 8. Sharing does not have to be perfect

It is easy to delay sharing because materials are not “finished”.

---

**Minimum (do this):**
- share something usable  

---

**If you have time:**
- improve documentation  

---

**Strong practice:**
- refine materials over time  

---

📌 **Key takeaway**  
Sharing something useful now is often more valuable than waiting for something perfect.

---

## Common mistakes

- Not sharing materials at all  
- Sharing without clear licensing  
- Sharing without context or instructions  
- Providing materials that are hard to edit  
- Waiting for materials to be perfect before sharing
