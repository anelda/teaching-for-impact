## Why this matters

In Module 1, you designed training that works **with you as the facilitator**.

But for your materials to be reused:

- by your future self  
- by colleagues  
- in different contexts

...they need to work without relying on your explanation, memory, or original context.

If your materials depend on:

- your explanations  
- your assumptions  
- your specific context  

then others (and even you, later) may struggle to use them.

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“This works when I teach it.”*  

    to:

    *“This works without me—and can be used by others.”*

    👉 Materials become more reusable when they no longer depend on your presence, assumptions, or explanations.

---

## Core concept

In this section, accessibility and usability are about **use beyond your immediate setting**.

!!! abstract "Accessible"
    Others can obtain and open your materials.

!!! abstract "Usable"
    Others can understand and use your materials without needing additional explanation from you.

---

### Important distinction

- Earlier sections focused on supporting learners during training  
- This section focuses on making materials usable without facilitation  

---

### Key question

> Could someone else use this resource in a different context, without needing you to explain it?

---

## A note on expectations

You do not need to do everything in this section.

Focus on:

- what is realistic  
- what will make the biggest difference

## Practical guidance

### 1. Make materials usable without relying on your explanation

Many materials rely on explanation that is not written down.

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- write clear instructions  
- state expected outputs  

---

**If you have time:**

- add examples  
- clarify common points of confusion  

---

**Strong practice:**

- anticipate questions  
- design materials that can be used independently  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Assuming instructions are clear because they make sense to you.

---

### 2. Ensure materials can be accessed in different environments

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- use formats that work on common devices  

---

**If you have time:**

- provide downloadable versions  
- reduce file sizes  

---

**Strong practice:**

- enable offline use  
- provide alternative formats  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    If materials cannot be accessed, they cannot be used.

---

### 3. Use formats that are easy to edit and adapt

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- use editable formats  

---

**If you have time:**

- structure content so sections can be modified  

---

**Strong practice:**

- design modular materials  

---

🔎 **Modular materials**  

Materials designed so parts can be reused, adapted, or reorganised independently.

---

### 4. Make assumptions visible

Your materials may assume:

- prior knowledge  
- access to tools  
- familiarity with concepts  

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- state prerequisites  

---

**If you have time:**

- explain required tools or alternatives  

---

**Strong practice:**

- highlight where adaptation is needed  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Unstated assumptions are one of the biggest barriers to reuse.

---

### 5. Ensure clarity and consistency

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- use simple, clear language  

---

**If you have time:**

- explain key terms  
- improve structure  

---

**Strong practice:**

- standardise terminology  
- refine flow across activities  

---

### 6. Test for reuse (not just delivery)

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- review your materials from another perspective  

---

**If you have time:**

- ask someone to review them  

---

**Strong practice:**

- ask someone to use them without your help  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Testing materials only while you are present to explain them.

---


### 7. Design for transfer across contexts

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- avoid unnecessary context-specific detail  

---

**If you have time:**

- separate core content from examples  

---

**Strong practice:**

- indicate where adaptation is expected  

---

### 8. Not everything should be generic

Making materials reusable does not mean removing all specificity.

Some content should remain:

- specialised  
- context-specific  
- grounded in real examples  

---

**Minimum (do this):**

- keep meaningful examples  

---

**If you have time:**

- indicate what can be changed  

---

**Strong practice:**

- separate structure from context  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Design for adaptability, not genericity.

---

!!! warning "Common mistakes"

    - Designing materials that depend on facilitation  
    - Leaving assumptions implicit  
    - Using formats that cannot be edited  
    - Over-generalising content  
    - Not testing usability

## Optional deeper insight

Usability determines whether materials can be reused.

Making assumptions visible and improving clarity:

- reduces barriers  
- supports independence  
- increases impact  

Designing for others also improves your own future use.
