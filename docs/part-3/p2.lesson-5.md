## Core concepts

### Publishing for reuse

!!! abstract "Publishing for reuse"
    Making materials available in ways that allow others to find, access, understand, and adapt them.

Publishing is not only about uploading files. It involves:

- making materials findable  
- making permissions clear  
- providing enough context for use  
- enabling adaptation  

Design implication: sharing only has impact if others can actually use what you share.

---

### Sustainability

!!! abstract "Sustainability"
    Ensuring materials remain usable, adaptable, and relevant over time.

Sustainability includes:

- updating materials  
- tracking changes  
- enabling others to contribute or adapt  

Design implication: design for ongoing use, not one-time sharing.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Decide what to share

You do not need to share everything.

Focus on materials that:

- are complete enough to be useful  
- can be understood without your explanation  
- have value beyond your immediate context  

Start small and build over time.

---

### Step 2 — Choose where to publish

Select a location where others can reliably access your materials.

Common options include:

- institutional repositories  
- open platforms (e.g. Zenodo, Figshare)  
- project or organisational websites  
- shared folders with clear structure  

Prioritise:

- stability  
- accessibility  
- ease of sharing  

---

### Step 3 — Make permissions clear

Others need to know what they are allowed to do.

Choose and include a license that reflects your intent.

For example:

- **CC BY** → reuse, adapt, and share with attribution  
- **CC BY-SA** → adaptations must be shared under the same license  
- **CC BY-NC** → restricts commercial use  
- **CC BY-ND** → allows reuse but not modification  

Ask:

- Do I want others to adapt this?  
- Do I want to restrict certain uses?  

Your license determines how your work can be used.

---

### Step 4 — Provide context for use

Your materials should not depend on your explanation.

Include:

- purpose of the resource  
- intended audience  
- instructions for use  

Where possible:

- include facilitator notes  
- indicate how materials can be adapted  

---

### Step 5 — Include attribution and sources

If you reused or adapted materials:

- credit original creators  
- indicate what you changed  

This supports transparency and reuse.

---

### Step 6 — Make materials easy to use and adapt

Improve usability by:

- sharing editable formats  
- organising files clearly  
- structuring materials into reusable parts  

Avoid formats that limit reuse.

---

### Step 7 — Track versions and updates

Your materials may evolve.

Support this by:

- noting version numbers or dates  
- keeping updated versions  
- incorporating feedback  

Versioning supports ongoing improvement.

---

### Step 8 — Share early and improve over time

Do not wait for materials to be perfect.

Instead:

- share something usable  
- improve based on feedback  
- refine over time  

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How will you approach sharing your materials?

- Share minimally (limited reuse potential)  
- Share with basic structure and context  
- Share intentionally to support reuse, adaptation, and sustainability  

Your choice affects impact, reach, and long-term value.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team has developed a training activity on climate data.

**Decision:**  
How should they share it?

**Action:**  
They:

- select one activity to share  
- upload it to an open repository  
- include a CC BY license  
- provide instructions and context  
- share an editable version  

They also document changes for future updates.

**Outcome:**  
The material can be found, used, adapted, and improved over time.
