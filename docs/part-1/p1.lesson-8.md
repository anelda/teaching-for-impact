## Why this matters

In the previous section, you designed opportunities for practice, feedback, and iteration.

This section focuses on how to **recognise learning and understand impact**—so that your training leads to meaningful improvement over time.

Training is often evaluated using:

- attendance  
- satisfaction (“Did participants enjoy it?”)  

These can be useful, but they do not tell you:

- whether learning happened  
- whether participants can apply what they learned  
- whether the training made a difference  

Effective training requires:

- assessment to understand learning  
- evaluation to understand impact  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“Did the training run?”*  
    to:  
    *“Did learning happen, and did it lead to meaningful change?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Assessment and evaluation

!!! abstract "Assessment and evaluation"
    Assessment focuses on what learners can do, while evaluation focuses on whether the training led to meaningful change.

- **Assessment** — what learners know or can do  
- **Evaluation** — whether the training achieved its intended outcomes  

Design implication: use assessment to support learning, and evaluation to understand impact.

---

### Alignment in assessment

Assessment should reflect your intended outcomes.

This means:

- learners demonstrate the skills or knowledge defined in your outcomes  
- assessment tasks match real-world use  

Design implication: if assessment does not match outcomes, you cannot accurately measure learning.

---

### Indicators and evidence

Indicators are observable signs that learning or change has occurred.

Evidence is how you recognise those indicators.

Design implication: define a small number of meaningful indicators and collect evidence that reflects real use.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Define what success looks like

Start with your outcomes and ask:

- What would learners be able to do if learning is successful?  

Define:

- 2–3 clear indicators of success  
- observable actions or outputs  

---

### Step 2 — Design aligned assessment

For each outcome, define how learners will demonstrate it.

Examples:

- completing a task  
- producing an output  
- applying a method  

Check:

- If learners complete this assessment, does it demonstrate the outcome?  

---

### Step 3 — Use formative and summative assessment

Include:

- formative assessment (during learning) to support improvement  
- summative assessment (at the end) to understand achievement  

Prioritise formative approaches where possible.

---

### Step 4 — Collect useful evidence

Use simple methods such as:

- observation (what learners do)  
- outputs (what they produce)  
- short feedback or reflection  

Focus on:

- usefulness  
- feasibility  

Avoid collecting data that will not be used.

---

### Step 5 — Focus on signal, not noise

Prioritise information that reflects real learning or change.

Examples:

- signal: learners successfully apply a method  
- noise: high attendance or positive satisfaction alone  

Choose data that helps you make decisions.

---

### Step 6 — Use evaluation to improve

Use what you learn to:

- refine your training design  
- identify gaps  
- improve future sessions  

Evaluation is part of an ongoing improvement process.

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much effort will you invest in meaningful assessment and evaluation?

- Minimal (basic attendance and satisfaction)  
- Moderate (some assessment of learning)  
- Focused and intentional (clear indicators, aligned assessment, useful evidence)  

Your choice affects how well you understand and improve your training.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is running training on analysing climate data for researchers.

**Decision:**  
Should they rely on attendance and feedback forms, or define clear indicators of learning?

**Action:**  
They choose to:

- define indicators (e.g. ability to analyse a dataset)  
- design an assessment task aligned with this outcome  
- observe participant outputs during the session  
- collect short feedback on challenges and application  

They focus on a small set of meaningful data.

**Outcome:**  
They can clearly see whether participants achieved the intended outcomes and use this information to improve future training.
