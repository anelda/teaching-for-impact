## Why this matters

In the previous section, you mapped your training as an intervention within a broader system.

This section focuses on one of the most important parts of that system: **your learners**.

Training is most effective when it aligns with how people actually live, work, and learn.

Well-designed training does not start with content—it starts with understanding:

- who your learners are  
- what shapes their participation  
- what knowledge they already bring  

When you design with learners in mind, your training becomes:

- more relevant  
- easier to apply  
- more likely to lead to meaningful change  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What should I teach?”*  
    to:  
    *“Who are my learners, and what is possible in their context?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Primary and secondary audiences

!!! abstract "Primary and secondary audiences"
    Primary audiences are the direct participants in your training. Secondary audiences are those they go on to support or train.

Your design should account for both:

- immediate learning (primary audience)  
- downstream impact (secondary audience)  

This is especially important when:

- participants act as trainers or intermediaries  
- training is expected to scale or be reused  

---

### Needs assessment

!!! abstract "Needs assessment"
    A simple process to understand what learners need, already know, and what constraints they face.

A needs assessment helps you:

- avoid incorrect assumptions  
- identify relevant content  
- design for real constraints  

It does not need to be complex. Simple approaches are often sufficient.

---

### Context shapes learning

Learning does not happen in isolation.

Key factors include:

- access (devices, connectivity, space)  
- time (availability, competing priorities)  
- language and literacy  
- social and cultural context  
- power dynamics  

These are not just limitations—they are **design conditions** that shape what is possible.

---

### Lived experience and mental models

!!! abstract "Mental models"
    The existing understanding learners use to interpret new information.

Learners bring:

- prior knowledge  
- lived experience  
- context-specific understanding  

This matters because:

- new learning builds on what already exists  
- misconceptions can affect understanding  
- local knowledge can strengthen relevance  

Recognising this allows you to design training that connects with what learners already know.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Identify your audiences

Clarify:

- Who are your primary participants?  
- Who are the secondary audiences they influence?  

---

### Step 2 — Understand learner realities

Gather information about:

- access (devices, connectivity)  
- time constraints  
- language and communication  
- social and cultural context  

---

### Step 3 — Conduct a simple needs assessment

Use:

- short conversations  
- informal interviews  
- quick surveys  
- co-mapping exercises  

Focus on:

- what learners already know  
- what they need to do  
- what challenges they face  

---

### Step 4 — Identify barriers and opportunities

Consider:

- What might prevent participation or success?  
- What existing strengths can you build on?  

---

### Step 5 — Design with learners, not just for them

Where possible:

- involve learners in shaping content  
- use examples from their context  
- validate different forms of knowledge  

---

### Decision point

> How much do you need to adapt your training to fit your learners’ realities?

- Minimal adaptation (aligned context)  
- Moderate adaptation (some constraints)  
- Significant redesign (highly constrained or different context)  

Your answer determines how you prioritise content, format, and delivery.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing training for early-career researchers who will train community members on climate data tools.

**Decision:**  
How should the training account for both researchers (primary audience) and community members (secondary audience)?

**Action:**  
They conduct a simple needs assessment and find:

- researchers are comfortable with technical tools  
- community members have limited access and prefer practical examples  

They design training that:

- prepares researchers to adapt content  
- includes low-tech alternatives  
- emphasises facilitation skills  

**Outcome:**  
Researchers are able to translate training effectively for community contexts, improving reach and impact.
