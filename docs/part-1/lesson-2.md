## Why this matters

Training often fails not because the content is wrong, but because it is **misaligned with learners’ realities**.

Common problems include:

- assuming too much prior knowledge  
- ignoring constraints (time, access, language)  
- designing for an “average learner” who does not exist  
- overlooking local knowledge and lived experience  

Effective training starts with understanding:

- **who your learners really are**
- **what shapes their ability to participate**
- **what knowledge they already bring**

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“What should I teach?”*  

    to:

    *“Who are my learners, and what is possible in their context?”*

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway" 
    Design for real learners in real contexts—not an imagined “average” participant.

---

## Who are your learners?

In this programme, we distinguish between:

### Primary audience

The people directly participating in your training.

### Secondary audience

The people your participants will go on to support or train.

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Your design must consider both immediate learning (primary audience) and downstream impact (secondary audience).

---

### Why this matters

Your design decisions must account for:

- immediate learning needs (primary audience)  
- downstream impact (secondary audience)  

This is especially important when:

- participants act as trainers, facilitators, or intermediaries  
- training is expected to scale or be reused  

---

## Moving beyond assumptions

Designing based on assumptions leads to:

- inaccessible content  
- unrealistic expectations  
- low engagement  

Instead, you need a **needs assessment**.

---

!!! abstract "Needs assessment"
    A simple process to understand what learners need, already know, and what constraints they face.

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Designing training based on what you think learners need, rather than what they actually need.

---

## Participatory needs assessment (practical approach)

You do not need complex research methods.

Simple approaches include:

- conversations with potential learners  
- observing real contexts  
- short surveys or informal interviews  
- co-mapping challenges and needs  

---

### What to look for

- What do learners already know?
- What do they need to be able to do?
- What constraints do they face?
- What motivates them to participate?

## Understanding context (beyond the classroom)

Learning does not happen in isolation.

Key contextual factors include:

---

### Access

- Devices (phones, laptops, shared devices)  
- Connectivity (stable, intermittent, offline)  
- Physical space (quiet, shared, mobile)  

---

### Time

- Work schedules  
- Care responsibilities  
- Competing priorities  

---

### Language and literacy

- Language of instruction vs home language  
- Technical vocabulary  
- Reading and writing levels  

---

### Social and cultural context

- Norms around participation  
- Authority and hierarchy  
- Gender dynamics  
- Community expectations  

---

### Power dynamics

- Who feels able to speak?  
- Who is excluded or marginalised?  
- Who is seen as “expert”?  

---

### Why this matters

These factors shape:
- who participates  
- how they participate  
- what learning is possible  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Context determines what is possible in learning—not just what is desirable.

---

## Designing for variability (not the average)

There is no “typical learner”.

Instead of designing for an average:

- design for **range and diversity**

---

### Practical strategies

- provide multiple ways to engage (discussion, writing, practice)  
- allow flexible pacing where possible  
- use examples relevant to different contexts  
- reduce unnecessary complexity  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Designing for a single “typical” learner who does not exist.

---

## Lived experience as knowledge

Participants are not empty containers.

They bring:

- practical experience  
- local knowledge  
- contextual understanding  

This is often:

- undervalued  
- excluded from formal training  

---

### Why this matters

Recognising lived experience:

- increases relevance  
- improves engagement  
- supports co-creation  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Learners’ lived experience is a resource—not a barrier.

---

### Practical implications

- ask participants to share experiences  
- use real-world examples from their contexts  
- validate different forms of knowledge  

---

## Barriers to participation and learning

Common barriers include:

- lack of access (devices, internet)  
- limited time  
- unfamiliar language or terminology  
- lack of confidence  
- exclusion due to power dynamics  

---

### Your role

You cannot remove all barriers, but you can:

- anticipate them  
- reduce them where possible  
- design around them  

---

## Psychological safety and participation

For learning to happen, participants need to feel:

- safe to ask questions  
- safe to make mistakes  
- safe to contribute  

---

!!! abstract "Psychological safety"
    A learning environment where participants feel comfortable contributing without fear of embarrassment or negative consequences.

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Expecting participation without creating conditions where people feel safe to contribute.

---

### Risks to psychological safety

- fear of being wrong  
- perceived hierarchy (trainer vs learner)  
- stereotype threat (fear of confirming negative assumptions)  

---

### Practical strategies

- normalise not knowing  
- encourage questions and discussion  
- avoid putting individuals on the spot  
- create small group interactions  

---

## Mental models and prior knowledge

Learners interpret new information through what they already know. These internal frameworks are called **mental models**.

---

!!! abstract "Mental models"
    The existing understanding or assumptions that learners use to make sense of new information.

---

### Why this matters

- New concepts are easier to learn when linked to existing knowledge  
- Misconceptions can block learning  

---

### Practical implications

- start from what learners already understand  
- use familiar examples  
- surface and address misconceptions  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    New learning builds on what learners already believe—whether correct or not.

---

## What this means in practice

Before designing content, you should be able to answer:

- Who are my learners (really)?
- What constraints shape their participation?
- What knowledge do they already have?
- What barriers might prevent success?
- What assumptions am I making?

---

!!! warning "Common mistakes"

    - Designing for an “average learner”  
    - Ignoring contextual constraints  
    - Overloading participants with unfamiliar concepts  
    - Treating learners as passive recipients  
    - Ignoring lived experience and local knowledge

## Optional deeper insight

This section draws on:

- Constructivist learning theory (learning builds on prior knowledge)  
- Situated learning (Lave & Wenger, 1991)  
- Communities of practice  
- Epistemic justice (Fricker, 2007)  

These perspectives emphasise that:
- knowledge is shaped by context  
- learning is social and participatory  
- different forms of knowledge should be recognised and valued
