## Why this matters

Even with clear outcomes and good intentions, training often fails at the point of **activity design**.

Common issues include:
- activities that are passive or unclear  
- tasks that do not support learning outcomes  
- experiences that do not fit the context  
- limited opportunities for participation  

Effective training depends on **what learners actually do**, not what you present.

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“What content should I include?”*  

    to:

    *“What should learners do to achieve the outcomes?”*

---

## From outcomes to activities

Your activities should directly support your outcomes.

---

### Key question

> If learners complete this activity, will they achieve the intended outcome?

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Designing activities that are engaging but not aligned with outcomes.

---

### Example

**Outcome:**  
Interpret climate data to identify risks  

**Aligned activity:**  
Analyse a dataset and identify key risks  

**Misaligned activity:**  
Listen to a lecture about climate data  

---

## Active vs passive learning

---

!!! abstract "Active learning"
    Learning through doing, discussing, and applying.

!!! abstract "Passive learning"
    Learning through listening, reading, or watching without active engagement—often leading to weaker understanding and retention.

### Passive approaches

- listening to lectures  
- reading without engagement  
- watching demonstrations  

---

### Active approaches

- solving problems  
- analysing real examples  
- discussing and explaining  
- applying knowledge in context  

---

### Why this matters

Active learning:

- improves understanding  
- strengthens retention  
- supports real-world application  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Learning depends on what learners do—not what you present.

---

## Designing for participation

Participation is not automatic—it must be designed.

---

### Barriers to participation

- lack of confidence  
- unclear instructions  
- power dynamics  
- language barriers  
- time pressure  

---

### Strategies for participation

- use small group work  
- provide clear, simple instructions  
- allow time for thinking before responding  
- include multiple ways to contribute (spoken, written, practical)  
- create psychologically safe environments  

---

## Structuring effective activities

A well-designed activity typically includes:

---

### 1. Clear objective

What should learners achieve?

---

### 2. Instructions

What should learners do?

- clear  
- concise  
- step-by-step  

---

### 3. Materials

What do they need?

- tools  
- data  
- examples  

---

### 4. Time

How long will it take?

---

### 5. Output

What will learners produce?

---

### 6. Feedback

How will they know if they succeeded?

## Scaffolding and sequencing

Learning should be structured in a way that:

- builds progressively  
- supports learners as they develop skills  

!!! abstract "Scaffolding"
    Providing support early and gradually removing it.

!!! abstract "Sequencing"
    Structuring learning so that each step builds on the previous one, moving from simple to more complex tasks.

---

### Scaffolding

Provide support early:

- examples  
- guidance  
- structured steps  

Gradually reduce support:

- encourage independence  
- increase complexity  

---

### Sequencing

Order activities so that:

- simple concepts come before complex ones  
- foundational skills are developed first  
- learning builds logically  

---

## Productive failure and exploration

Allow learners to:

- try  
- struggle  
- explore  

---

### Why this matters

Struggle (when supported):

- deepens understanding  
- reveals misconceptions  
- supports problem-solving skills  

---

### Important balance

- Too much struggle → frustration  
- Too little challenge → limited learning  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Removing all difficulty or providing too much guidance.

---

## Designing for co-creation

Activities can also support:

- shared knowledge creation  
- collaboration  
- community input  

---

### Examples

- co-developing solutions  
- sharing local examples  
- building resources together  

---

### Why this matters

Co-creation:

- increases relevance  
- values participant knowledge  
- supports ownership  

---

## Adapting activities for constraints

Your activities must work in your context.

---

### Consider

- available time  
- access to technology  
- group size  
- participant experience  

---

### Practical adaptations

- simplify tasks  
- reduce dependency on technology  
- use locally available resources  
- adjust time and scope  

---

## ⚙️ What this means in practice

When designing an activity, ask:

- What will learners DO?  
- How does this support the outcome?  
- Is this realistic in the context?  
- How will learners receive feedback?  

---

!!! warning "Common mistakes"

    - Designing activities that are too complex  
    - Giving unclear instructions  
    - Overloading participants  
    - Failing to connect activities to outcomes  
    - Ignoring contextual constraints
