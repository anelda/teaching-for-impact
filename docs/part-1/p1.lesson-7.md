## Why this matters

In the previous section, you designed learning activities that support participation and engagement.

This section focuses on how learning becomes **usable and transferable**—through practice, feedback, and iteration.

Learning does not happen through exposure alone. Participants need:

- opportunities to practice  
- feedback to improve  
- time to revise and strengthen their work  

Without this:

- learning remains theoretical  
- skills are not developed  
- participants struggle to apply what they learned  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“Did I deliver the training?”*  
    to:  
    *“Did participants practice, receive feedback, and improve?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Practice

!!! abstract "Practice"
    Opportunities for learners to apply knowledge and develop skills in realistic contexts.

Effective practice:

- is aligned with outcomes  
- reflects real-world use  
- is repeated and supported  

Design implication: practice is where learning becomes usable.

---

### Feedback

!!! abstract "Feedback"
    Information that helps learners understand what they are doing well and how to improve.

Effective feedback is:

- specific  
- timely  
- actionable  

Design implication: feedback should guide improvement, not just evaluate performance.

---

### Iteration

Learning improves through cycles of:

- attempting  
- receiving feedback  
- revising  

This creates a progression from initial attempts to improved performance.

Design implication: build opportunities for revision into your design.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Design meaningful practice

Ensure learners:

- apply key skills  
- work on realistic tasks  
- connect practice to outcomes  

Include different forms of practice:

- guided (with support)  
- independent (with reduced support)  
- collaborative (with peers)  

---

### Step 2 — Integrate feedback into activities

Plan feedback as part of the activity:

- include checkpoints during tasks  
- define what good performance looks like  
- allow time for feedback during or immediately after practice  

Use different sources:

- facilitator feedback  
- peer feedback  

---

### Step 3 — Make feedback useful

Ensure feedback:

- focuses on specific aspects of performance  
- includes suggestions for improvement  
- is given at the right time  

For example:

- instead of general comments, highlight what worked and what can improve  

---

### Step 4 — Build in iteration

Create opportunities for learners to:

- revise their work  
- improve based on feedback  
- try again  

Simple approaches include:

- multiple versions (e.g. first attempt → revised version)  
- revisiting tasks across sessions  

---

### Step 5 — Enable peer learning

Support participants to learn from each other:

- include small group work  
- design peer review activities  
- encourage sharing of approaches and solutions  

As a facilitator:

- guide discussions  
- ensure inclusive participation  
- support constructive feedback  

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much opportunity will learners have to practice, receive feedback, and improve?

- Limited practice with minimal feedback  
- Practice with some feedback but limited revision  
- Structured practice with feedback and multiple opportunities to improve  

Your choice affects how well learning translates into real-world capability.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing training for researchers to analyse and communicate climate data.

**Decision:**  
Should participants complete a single activity, or have opportunities to improve their work?

**Action:**  
They design a sequence where participants:

- complete an initial analysis (first attempt)  
- receive peer and facilitator feedback  
- revise their analysis  

They also include a follow-up discussion to reflect on improvements.

**Outcome:**  
Participants produce stronger analyses, understand their mistakes, and gain confidence in applying the method independently.
