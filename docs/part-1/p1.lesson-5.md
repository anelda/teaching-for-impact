## Why this matters

In the previous section, you defined outcomes and aligned your training design to support them.

This section focuses on how to design **with people and within real-world constraints**—so that your training is relevant, usable, and sustainable.

Training is often designed:

- by experts  
- for learners  
- without meaningful input from those it aims to support  

This can result in training that is:

- difficult to apply  
- disconnected from real needs  
- hard to sustain  

At the same time, most training contexts include constraints:

- limited time and resources  
- uneven access to technology  
- social and institutional dynamics  

Effective training requires working with both:

- people (through co-design)  
- constraints (as part of the design process)  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What would be ideal?”*  
    to:  
    *“What is possible, meaningful, and sustainable in this context?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Co-design

!!! abstract "Co-design"
    A participatory approach where learners or stakeholders actively contribute to shaping the training.

Co-design involves participants in:

- defining problems  
- shaping priorities  
- designing activities  
- refining solutions  

Design implication: participants are contributors, not just recipients.

---

### Constraints

!!! abstract "Constraints"
    Real-world limits (time, resources, access, social dynamics) that shape what is possible.

Constraints are not obstacles to ignore—they are conditions to design within.

Design implication: effective training works within constraints, not ideal scenarios.

---

### Trade-offs

Design decisions often involve trade-offs between:

- depth and breadth  
- interactivity and time  
- complexity and accessibility  

Design implication: prioritisation is essential—focus on what matters most for learning.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Identify who is shaping the training

Clarify:

- Who is currently making design decisions?  
- Who should be involved but is not yet included?  

Aim to:

- involve participants early  
- create opportunities for input and influence  

---

### Step 2 — Identify key constraints

Map the most important constraints affecting your training:

- time (duration, availability)  
- technology (devices, connectivity)  
- resources (materials, funding)  
- social and cultural dynamics  

Focus on the constraints that most strongly affect what is possible.

---

### Step 3 — Design with participants

Involve participants in practical ways:

- discuss needs before finalising design  
- invite input on examples and activities  
- adapt content based on feedback  

Keep this manageable—co-design can be simple and iterative.

---

### Step 4 — Make explicit trade-offs

Ask:

- What matters most for achieving the intended outcomes?  

Then:

- prioritise essential content and activities  
- simplify or remove non-essential elements  

---

### Step 5 — Ensure ethical and meaningful participation

Check that your approach:

- gives participants real influence  
- recognises and values local knowledge  
- creates benefit for participants  

Avoid approaches where participants contribute without impact.

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much influence will participants have in shaping your training?

- Minimal input (feedback only)  
- Moderate input (some adaptation based on input)  
- Strong co-design (participants shape key decisions)  

Your choice affects relevance, ownership, and sustainability.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing training for community organisers working with climate data in low-resource settings.

**Decision:**  
How much should participants influence the training design, given time and resource constraints?

**Action:**  
They choose to:

- conduct short pre-training conversations to understand needs  
- invite participants to contribute local examples  
- adapt activities during delivery based on feedback  

They also prioritise low-tech approaches due to limited connectivity.

**Outcome:**  
The training reflects local realities, participants engage more actively, and the materials are reused and adapted in future contexts.
