## Why this matters

Training often focuses on:

- what content to include  
- how to present information  

But learning is not the same as exposure to content.

People do not learn effectively by:

- listening passively  
- reading large amounts of information  
- being told what to do  

Learning requires:

- active engagement  
- practice  
- connection to prior knowledge  
- time and reinforcement  

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“How do I present content?”*  

    to:

    *“How do people actually learn—and how can I support that?”*

---

## Learning as a cognitive process

Learning involves changes in the brain.

Two key components:

### Working memory

- Limited capacity  
- Handles new information  
- Easily overloaded  

### Long-term memory

- Stores knowledge and skills  
- Enables retrieval and application  

---

### Why this matters

If you overload working memory:

- learners become confused or disengaged  

If you support transfer to long-term memory:

- learning becomes usable and lasting  

---

## Cognitive load (practical concept)

!!! abstract "Cognitive load" 
    The amount of mental effort required to process information.

---

### Types of cognitive load

- **Intrinsic load** — complexity of the content  
- **Extraneous load** — unnecessary difficulty (poor design, unclear instructions)  
- **Germane load** — effort that supports learning  

---

### ⚙️ Practical implications

To support learning:
- reduce unnecessary complexity  
- simplify instructions  
- break content into manageable parts  
- focus attention on what matters  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Reduce unnecessary difficulty so learners can focus on learning.

---

## Retrieval practice: learning by recalling

Learning is strengthened when learners:

- actively recall information  
- apply it in practice  


!!! abstract "Retrieval practice"
    Strengthening learning by recalling and applying knowledge.

---

### Examples

- answering questions  
- explaining concepts  
- completing tasks  
- applying knowledge in context  

---

### Why this matters

Retrieval:

- strengthens memory  
- improves understanding  
- supports transfer to real-world use  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Learning improves when learners actively recall and use knowledge.

---


## Spaced learning and repetition

Learning improves when it is:

- spread over time  
- revisited multiple times  

!!! abstract "Spacing"
    Revisiting learning over time to strengthen retention.

---

### Why this matters

Single exposure is rarely enough.

Spacing and repetition:

- reinforce learning  
- reduce forgetting  

---

### Practical strategies

- revisit key ideas across sessions  
- include follow-up activities  
- encourage continued practice  

---

## Desirable difficulties

Some level of challenge supports learning.

---

### What this means

Learning is more effective when it requires effort:

- solving problems  
- making decisions  
- struggling (productively)  

---

### Balance is important

Too easy:

- little learning  

Too difficult:

- frustration and disengagement  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Making learning either too easy (no challenge) or too difficult (overload).

---

## Social learning

Learning is not only individual—it is social.

People learn through:

- discussion  
- collaboration  
- observing others  
- shared problem-solving  

---

### Communities of practice

Learning happens through participation in a community:

- sharing experiences  
- solving real problems  
- building knowledge together  

---

### Practical implications

- include group work and discussion  
- encourage peer feedback  
- create opportunities for collaboration  

---

## Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

!!! abstract "Zone of Proximal Development"
    The space where learners can succeed with support but not yet independently.

---

### Why this matters

Learning is most effective when:

- tasks are slightly beyond current ability  
- support (scaffolding) is provided  

---

### Scaffolding strategies

- provide guidance and examples  
- break tasks into steps  
- gradually reduce support  

---

## Common misconceptions about learning

---

### “Learning styles”

There is little evidence that:

- teaching to specific “learning styles” improves learning  

---

### Passive learning

Listening or reading alone:

- does not lead to strong learning  

---

### More content = better learning

Overloading content:

- reduces understanding  
- limits retention  

---

## Applying learning principles in context

You do not need to apply all theories.

Focus on a few key principles:

- reduce cognitive overload  
- design for active participation  
- include retrieval and practice  
- support social learning  
- design within real constraints  

---

## What this means in practice

When designing activities, ask:

- What do learners DO?  
- How do they practice?  
- How do they apply knowledge?  
- How is learning reinforced over time?  

---

!!! warning "Common mistakes"

    - Overloading learners with information  
    - Relying on lectures or passive delivery  
    - Ignoring prior knowledge  
    - Designing activities without practice  
    - Failing to revisit key concepts
