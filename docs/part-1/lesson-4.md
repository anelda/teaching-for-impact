## Why this matters

Many training programmes fail because they are **not aligned**.

Common issues include:

- unclear or vague learning outcomes  
- activities that do not support intended outcomes  
- assessments that measure the wrong things  
- a disconnect between what is taught and what learners need  

As a result:

- participants may enjoy the training  
- but are unable to apply what they learned  

Effective training requires **intentional alignment** between:

- what you want learners to achieve  
- what they do during training  
- how you know they have succeeded  

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“What should I include?”*  

    to:

    *“What should learners be able to DO, and how will I support that?”*

---

## Core concept

!!! abstract "Outcomes"
    What learners are able to do after the training.

!!! abstract "Outputs"
    Immediate results of training (e.g. completed activities), not necessarily lasting change.

!!! abstract "Impact"
    Longer-term changes that result from applying what was learned.

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"  
    Design for outcomes (what learners can do), not just outputs (what they complete).

---

## Starting with impact (not content)

Training often starts with:

- topics  
- slides  
- materials  

Instead, start with:

> What should change as a result of this training?

---


## Backward design (practical approach)

!!! abstract "Backward design"
    Designing training by starting with outcomes, then defining evidence, and only then designing activities.

---

### In simple terms

> Outcomes → Evidence → Activities

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Start with what learners need to do—not what you want to teach.

---

### Why this matters

This prevents:

- content overload  
- irrelevant activities  
- disconnected learning experiences  

---

## Writing effective learning outcomes

Learning outcomes describe what learners will be able to **DO**.

---

### Strong vs weak outcomes

Weak:

- “Understand climate data”  
- “Be familiar with tools”

Strong:

- “Interpret climate data to identify local risks”  
- “Use [tool] to analyse and communicate findings”

---

### Characteristics of strong outcomes

- **Observable** — can be seen or demonstrated  
- **Actionable** — involve doing something  
- **Relevant** — connected to real-world use  
- **Realistic** — achievable within constraints  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    If you cannot observe it, you cannot assess it.

---

### Practical guidance

Use action verbs such as:

- analyse  
- design  
- interpret  
- apply  
- evaluate  

Avoid vague verbs such as:

- understand  
- know  
- learn

## Constructive alignment

Constructive alignment ensures that:

- outcomes, activities, and assessment all support each other  

---

### The three elements

1. **Outcomes** — what learners should achieve  
2. **Activities** — what learners do  
3. **Assessment** — how you know they succeeded  

---

### Alignment question

> If a learner completes the activity, does it help them achieve the outcome?

---

!!! warning "Common mistake" 
    Designing engaging activities that do not support the intended outcome.

---

## Diagnosing misalignment

Misalignment is very common.

Examples:

- Teaching theory but expecting practical application  
- Assessing knowledge recall when outcomes require skills  
- Activities that are engaging but not relevant  

---

### Practical diagnostic questions

- What is taught but not assessed?  
- What is assessed but not taught?  
- What activities do not contribute to outcomes?  

👉 You will apply this in [Template 6: Alignment Table](../activities/template_6_alignment.md).

## Designing for real-world application

Your outcomes should reflect:

- what learners will actually need to do  
- in their real contexts  

---

### Key considerations

- What tools or methods will they use?  
- What constraints will they face?  
- What decisions will they need to make?  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Learning transfers better when it resembles real-world use.

---

## Indicators and evidence of success

!!! abstract "Indicator"
    A clear, observable sign that learning or change has happened.

You will define and apply indicators in [Section 8](../part-1/lesson-8.md).

---

You need to define:

- how you will know if learning has occurred  

---

### Types of evidence

- observable actions (e.g. completing a task)  
- outputs (e.g. a design, analysis, or plan)  
- changes in behaviour or decision-making  

---

### Keep it simple

Focus on:

- 2–3 meaningful indicators  
- not complex measurement systems  

👉 You will apply this in [Template 8: Feedback & Evaluation Plan](../activities/template_8_evaluation.md).

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Measure what matters—not what is easiest to count.

---

## What this means in practice

Before designing activities, you should be able to answer:

- What should learners be able to DO?
- How will I recognise success?
- What activities will support this?
- Is this realistic in their context?

---

## Common mistakes

- Writing vague or unobservable outcomes  
- Designing activities before defining outcomes  
- Including too many outcomes  
- Misalignment between outcomes, activities, and assessment  
- Focusing on content coverage instead of learning

## Optional deeper insight

This section draws on:

- Backward design (Wiggins & McTighe, 2005)  
- Constructive alignment (Biggs, 1996)  
- Bloom’s Taxonomy (revised)  

These frameworks emphasise that:

- learning should be intentional and aligned  
- outcomes should guide design decisions  
- assessment should reflect meaningful learning
