## Why this matters

In the previous section, you explored how to co-design training with participants and work within constraints.

This section focuses on turning your design into **effective learning experiences**—where participants actively engage, contribute, and apply what they learn.

Training often breaks down at the activity level:

- activities are unclear or too complex  
- participation is limited  
- tasks do not support outcomes  
- activities do not fit the context  

Effective training depends on what learners **do**, not what you present.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What content should I include?”*  
    to:  
    *“What should learners do to achieve the outcomes?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Active learning

!!! abstract "Active learning"
    Learning through doing, discussing, and applying knowledge.

Active learning includes:

- solving problems  
- analysing examples  
- discussing ideas  
- applying knowledge in context  

Design implication: learning improves when participants actively engage with content.

---

### Activity structure

!!! abstract "Activity structure"
    A clear and consistent way of defining what learners will do, how, and why.

A well-structured activity includes:

- objective (what learners will achieve)  
- instructions (what to do)  
- materials (what is needed)  
- time (how long it takes)  
- output (what is produced)  
- feedback (how success is recognised)  

Design implication: clarity enables participation and reduces confusion.

---

### Scaffolding and sequencing

Learning is more effective when it is:

- supported early  
- structured progressively  

This involves:

- scaffolding (providing support, then reducing it)  
- sequencing (ordering activities from simple to more complex)  

Design implication: build confidence first, then increase independence and complexity.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Design activities from outcomes

For each outcome, define:

- what learners will do  
- how this supports the outcome  

Check:

- If learners complete this activity, will they achieve the outcome?  

---

### Step 2 — Structure each activity clearly

Define:

- objective  
- instructions (clear, step-by-step)  
- materials  
- time  
- output  
- feedback  

Ensure instructions are simple and unambiguous.

---

### Step 3 — Design for participation

Make participation intentional:

- use small group or pair work  
- allow time for thinking before responding  
- provide multiple ways to contribute (spoken, written, practical)  

Reduce barriers by:

- simplifying instructions  
- clarifying expectations  
- creating inclusive participation options  

---

### Step 4 — Apply scaffolding and sequencing

Support learners by:

- starting with guided tasks and examples  
- gradually increasing complexity  
- reducing support over time  

Sequence activities so that:

- foundational skills come first  
- later activities build on earlier ones  

---

### Step 5 — Adapt for context and co-creation

Ensure activities work in your context:

- simplify where needed  
- reduce reliance on technology  
- use locally relevant examples  

Where possible:

- invite participants to contribute examples  
- adapt activities based on input  
- create space for shared knowledge  

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How structured and participatory will your learning activities be?

- Loosely structured, facilitator-led  
- Moderately structured with some participation  
- Highly structured, participant-driven and interactive  

Your choice affects clarity, engagement, and learning outcomes.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing a session to help researchers analyse and interpret climate data.

**Decision:**  
Should they use a lecture-based approach or a structured, hands-on activity?

**Action:**  
They design an activity that includes:

- a clear objective (analyse a dataset)  
- step-by-step instructions  
- a real dataset relevant to participants  
- small group work  
- a defined output (identified risks and insights)  
- feedback through group discussion  

They also begin with a guided example and then move to independent work.

**Outcome:**  
Participants actively engage, apply the method, and are able to use it independently after the session.
