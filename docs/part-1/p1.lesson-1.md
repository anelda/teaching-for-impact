## Why this matters

Many training programmes are designed as **events**:

- a workshop  
- a course  
- a set of materials  

But training does not exist in isolation.

Training is an **intervention in a system**:

- it interacts with institutions, communities, resources, and constraints  
- it is shaped by power, access, and context  
- its outcomes depend on much more than what happens in the session  

If you design training without understanding the system it sits in, you risk:

- overestimating impact  
- ignoring constraints  
- designing something that cannot work in practice  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What should I teach?”*  
    to:  
    *“What change is possible in this system—and how can training contribute?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Training as an intervention

!!! abstract "Intervention"
    A deliberate action designed to create change.

Training is one type of intervention. It:

- introduces new knowledge or skills  
- creates opportunities for practice  
- can shift behaviours, decisions, or relationships  

However, training alone rarely creates impact.

Impact depends on:

- whether learners can apply what they learn  
- whether the environment supports change  
- whether the training aligns with real needs and constraints  

---

### Systems thinking (practical)

A system includes:

- people (learners, facilitators, organisations)  
- resources (time, tools, infrastructure)  
- relationships (power, trust, collaboration)  
- external factors (policy, culture, environment)  

In practice, your training sits within this system—it does not control it.

This has two important implications:

- your design must work **within real constraints**  
- your training must connect to **how people actually operate**  

Co-creation is one way to do this.

Instead of designing *for* participants, you design *with* them:

- involving participants in defining problems  
- adapting content based on feedback  
- building on local knowledge and experience  

This increases:

- relevance  
- uptake  
- sustainability  

---

### Theory of Change

A **Theory of Change** is a simple explanation of how your training leads to outcomes and impact.

Structure:

- Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact  

This helps you:

- make assumptions visible  
- identify weak points  
- design more realistic training  

---

### Positionality, roles, and power

!!! abstract "Positionality"
    How your background, role, and perspective influence how you design and deliver training.

Training is not neutral.

Your positionality shapes:

- how you define the problem  
- whose knowledge is valued  
- how participants engage  

Your role in the training also matters.

You may act as:

- Facilitator — guiding learning rather than delivering content  
- Co-learner — learning alongside participants  
- Co-designer — shaping the training with participants  
- Connector — linking people, ideas, and resources  

These roles influence:

- who contributes knowledge  
- how decisions are made  
- what kinds of learning are possible  

Key questions to consider:

- Who defines the problem?  
- Who benefits from this training?  
- Who is excluded?

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Map your system

Identify:

- Who is involved? (actors)  
- What resources exist or are missing?  
- What constraints matter most?  
- What external factors influence success?  

---

### Step 2 — Define intended change

Be specific:

- What should participants do differently?  
- What will change after the training?  

---

### Step 3 — Build a simple Theory of Change

Link:

- what you do → what participants gain → what changes  

---

### Step 4 — Identify assumptions and risks

Ask:

- What must be true for this to work?  
- What is outside your control?  

---

### Step 5 — Reflect on your role

Decide:

- Are you delivering, facilitating, or co-creating?  
- How will participants shape the process?  

---

### Decision point

> What level of change is realistic in your context?

- Individual skills only?  
- Changes in practice?  
- Institutional or community impact?  

Your answer determines how you design the training.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing climate data training for community organisers in a low-resource setting.

**Decision:**  
Should they focus on advanced tools or practical, low-tech approaches—and how much should participants shape the design?

**Action:**  
They map the system and identify:

- limited internet access  
- varied technical skills  
- strong local knowledge  

They choose to:

- co-design parts of the training with participants  
- use offline tools  
- include locally relevant examples  

They also adjust their role from instructor to facilitator.

**Outcome:**  
Participants are able to apply what they learn immediately, and the training is adapted for future use in similar contexts.

## Key takeaways

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Training works within systems—its success depends on context, not just content.
