## Why this matters

In the previous section, you analysed your learners, their contexts, and the knowledge they bring.

This section focuses on how learning actually happens—so that your design decisions support it.

Training is not effective simply because content is delivered. Learning happens when participants:

- engage actively  
- connect new ideas to what they already know  
- practice and apply knowledge  
- revisit and reinforce learning over time  

If you design without understanding how learning works, you risk:

- overwhelming learners  
- creating passive experiences  
- limiting retention and real-world application  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“How do I present content?”*  
    to:  
    *“How do people actually learn—and how do I design for that?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Cognitive load

!!! abstract "Cognitive load"
    The amount of mental effort required to process information.

Working memory is limited. When too much new information is introduced at once, learners struggle to process it.

This means:

- complex content must be broken into manageable parts  
- instructions must be clear and simple  
- unnecessary complexity should be removed  

Design implication: reduce avoidable difficulty so learners can focus on what matters.

---

### Retrieval and practice

!!! abstract "Retrieval practice"
    Strengthening learning by actively recalling and applying knowledge.

Learning becomes durable when learners:

- recall information (not just re-read it)  
- apply it in meaningful ways  
- explain or use it in context  

Design implication: build activities where learners *do something* with knowledge, not just receive it.

---

### Spacing and reinforcement

Learning strengthens when it is revisited over time.

Single exposure is rarely enough.

Effective designs include:

- revisiting key ideas across sessions  
- follow-up activities  
- opportunities to apply learning in different contexts  

Design implication: plan for reinforcement, not just delivery.

---

### Social learning

Learning is shaped by interaction with others.

People learn through:

- discussion  
- collaboration  
- observing peers  
- shared problem-solving  

Design implication: include structured opportunities for interaction and peer learning.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Reduce unnecessary cognitive load

- break content into smaller segments  
- use clear, simple instructions  
- avoid introducing multiple new ideas at once  

---

### Step 2 — Design for active learning

For each part of your training, ask:

- What are learners doing?  
- How are they applying knowledge?  

Include:

- short exercises  
- problem-solving tasks  
- explanation or reflection activities  

---

### Step 3 — Build in retrieval

Create opportunities where learners:

- recall key ideas  
- answer questions  
- apply concepts without prompts  

---

### Step 4 — Plan for reinforcement

- revisit key ideas across sessions  
- include follow-up tasks  
- encourage continued practice beyond the session  

---

### Step 5 — Use social learning intentionally

- include discussion prompts  
- design group activities  
- encourage peer feedback  

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How much active learning and practice will you include—and what will learners actually do during your training?

- Mostly content delivery with limited activity  
- Balanced content and practice  
- Practice-focused, highly interactive design  

Your choice will directly affect how much learning is retained and applied.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing a session on using climate data tools for early-career researchers.

**Decision:**  
Should they prioritise covering many tools or focus on fewer tools with hands-on practice?

**Action:**  
They choose to:

- reduce the number of tools introduced  
- include guided exercises where learners use one tool  
- add a short reflection where learners explain how they would apply it  

They also include a follow-up activity for continued practice.

**Outcome:**  
Learners are able to use the tool independently and apply it in their work, rather than having only surface-level awareness of multiple tools.
