## Why this matters

Many training programmes are designed as **events**:

- a workshop
- a course
- a set of materials

But training does not exist in isolation.

Training is an **intervention in a system**:

- it interacts with institutions, communities, resources, and constraints
- it is shaped by power, access, and context
- its outcomes depend on much more than what happens in the session

!!! abstract "Intervention"
    A deliberate action designed to create change.

!!! abstract "System"
    The broader environment your training operates in, including people, resources, relationships, and constraints.

---

If you design training without understanding the system it sits in, you risk:

- overestimating impact
- ignoring constraints
- designing something that cannot work in practice

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“What should I teach?”*  

    to:

    *“What change is possible in this system—and how can training contribute?”*

---

## What is training as an intervention?

An intervention is a **deliberate action to create change**.

Training is one type of intervention. It:

- introduces new knowledge or skills
- creates opportunities for practice
- can shift behaviours, decisions, or relationships

However, training alone rarely creates impact.

Impact depends on:

- whether learners can **apply what they learn**
- whether the **environment supports change**
- whether the training aligns with **real needs and constraints**

---

## Thinking in systems (practical, not abstract)

A system includes:

- people (learners, facilitators, organisations)
- resources (time, tools, infrastructure)
- relationships (power, trust, collaboration)
- external factors (policy, culture, environment)

---

!!! warning "Common Mistake" 
    Designing training as if it exists independently of context.

---

### A simple way to map your system

Instead of trying to map everything, focus on:

- **Actors** — who is involved?
- **Inputs** — what resources are used?
- **Activities** — what happens during training?
- **Outputs** — what is produced immediately?
- **Outcomes** — what changes for participants?
- **External influences** — what affects success beyond your control?

## From activity to impact: Theory of Change

!!! abstract "Theory of Change"
    A simple explanation of how your training is expected to lead to outcomes and impact.

A simple structure:

> Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    If you cannot explain how your training leads to change, it is unlikely to create impact.

---

### Example

- Inputs: facilitators, materials, time  
- Activities: workshops, discussions, practice  
- Outputs: completed exercises, participation  
- Outcomes: improved skills, changed practices  
- Impact: better decision-making, community benefit  

---

### Why this matters

This helps you:

- make assumptions visible  
- identify weak points  
- design more realistic training

## Roles in training: beyond “the instructor”

In many contexts, the trainer is seen as:

- the expert
- the authority
- the person who delivers knowledge

In this programme, we expand that role.

You may act as:

- **Facilitator** — guiding learning, not just delivering content  
- **Co-learner** — learning alongside participants  
- **Co-designer** — shaping training with participants  
- **Connector** — linking people, ideas, and resources  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"

    Your role affects:

    - how participants engage  
    - who contributes knowledge  
    - what kinds of learning are possible  

---

## Power, positionality, and ethics

!!! abstract "Positionality"
    How your background, role, and perspective influence how you design and deliver training.

Training is not neutral.

Important questions include:

- Who defines the problem?
- Whose knowledge is valued?
- Who benefits from this training?
- Who is excluded?

Your **positionality** (your background, role, and perspective) shapes:

- how you design training  
- how participants experience it  

---

### Practical implications

- Recognise local and lived experience as valid knowledge  
- Avoid assuming your perspective is universal  
- Create space for participants to shape the process  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Assuming your perspective is neutral or universal.

---

## Co-creation: from delivery to shared ownership

Traditional training:

- designed by experts  
- delivered to participants  

Co-created training:

- designed **with** participants or communities  
- shaped by their needs and knowledge  
- shared ownership of outcomes  

---

### What co-creation can look like

- involving participants in defining problems  
- adapting content based on feedback  
- creating materials together  
- iterating based on real-world use  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"

    Co-creation:

    - increases relevance  
    - improves uptake  
    - supports sustainability  

👉 You will begin this process in [Template 3: Co-Design Plan](../activities/template_3_co_design.md).

## Iteration: training as an evolving process

Training is not finished when it is delivered.

Effective training:

- is tested in real contexts  
- adapted based on feedback  
- improved over time  

---

### A simple iteration cycle

1. Design (initial version)  
2. Test (with real participants)  
3. Gather feedback  
4. Adapt and improve  

---

### Versioning your training

⚙️ **In practice**

Think in versions:

- **v0** — current or initial design  
- **v1** — after core sessions  
- **v2** — after feedback and refinement  

---

## What this means in practice

Before designing content, you should be able to answer:

- What system does my training sit in?
- What change is realistic in this context?
- Who is involved, and who is missing?
- What assumptions am I making?
- Where are the biggest risks?

---

## Common mistakes

- Treating training as a one-off event  
- Ignoring system-level constraints  
- Assuming learning automatically leads to impact  
- Designing without understanding learners or context  
- Overestimating what training alone can achieve
