## Why this matters

Learning does not happen by exposure alone.

Participants need:

- opportunities to **practice**
- feedback to **improve**
- time to **iterate**

Many training programmes fail because:

- practice is limited or unrealistic  
- feedback is delayed or absent  
- there is no opportunity to improve  

As a result:

- learners leave with ideas, but not usable skills  

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“Did I deliver the training?”*  

    to:

    *“Did participants practice, receive feedback, and improve?”*

---

## Practice as the core of learning

Practice is where learning becomes usable.

---

### What effective practice looks like

- aligned with learning outcomes  
- realistic and relevant  
- repeated over time  
- supported with feedback  

---

### Examples

- applying a method to real data  
- designing a solution to a local problem  
- using a tool in a realistic scenario  

---

### Why this matters

Without practice:

- knowledge remains abstract  
- skills are not developed  

---

## Types of practice

---

### Guided practice

- learners receive support  
- instructions and examples are provided  

---

### Independent practice

- learners apply skills on their own  
- support is reduced  

---

### Collaborative practice

- learners work together  
- share ideas and approaches  

---

### Practical implication

Include a mix of:

- guided → independent → collaborative practice  

---

## Feedback: making learning visible


!!! abstract "Feedback"
    Information that helps learners understand what they are doing well and how to improve.

Feedback helps learners:

- understand what they are doing well  
- identify what needs improvement  
- adjust their approach  

---

### Types of feedback

- **Immediate feedback** — during or right after an activity  
- **Delayed feedback** — after reflection or review  
- **Peer feedback** — from other participants  
- **Facilitator feedback** — from you  

---

### What makes feedback effective

- specific  
- timely  
- actionable  
- focused on improvement  

---

### Example

Weak feedback:

- “Good job”

Strong feedback:

- “Your analysis identifies key risks clearly. To improve, explain how you selected these variables.”

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Feedback should help learners improve—not just judge performance.

---

## Designing feedback into activities

Feedback should not be an afterthought.

---

### ⚙️Practical strategies

- include checkpoints during activities  
- build in peer review  
- provide clear criteria for success  
- allow time for revision

## Peer learning and co-learning

Participants can learn from each other.

---

### Benefits

- exposure to different perspectives  
- shared problem-solving  
- reduced reliance on the facilitator  

---

### ⚙️ Practical strategies

- small group discussions  
- peer review sessions  
- collaborative activities  

---

### Facilitator role

- guide discussions  
- ensure inclusive participation  
- support constructive feedback  

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"
    Learning is strengthened when participants engage with each other.

---

## Iteration: improving through cycles

!!! abstract "Iteration"
    Improving work through repeated cycles of attempt, feedback, and revision.

Learning improves when participants:

- try  
- receive feedback  
- revise  

---

### Iteration cycle

1. Attempt  
2. Receive feedback  
3. Reflect  
4. Improve  

---

### Why this matters

Iteration:

- deepens understanding  
- builds confidence  
- leads to better outcomes  

---

## Designing for iteration

You need to create space for:

- multiple attempts  
- feedback loops  
- revision  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Not allowing time for learners to revise their work after feedback.

---

### ⚙️ Practical strategies

- revisit activities across sessions  
- include follow-up discussions  
- encourage participants to refine their work  

---

### Versioning

Encourage participants to work in versions:

- **v0** — initial attempt  
- **v1** — after feedback  
- **v2** — improved version  

---

## Feedback in co-creation contexts

When working with communities or participants:

- feedback should be mutual  
- learning should be shared  
- improvement should be collaborative  

---

### Ethical considerations

- respect participant contributions  
- avoid extractive feedback practices  
- ensure feedback benefits participants  

---

## ⚙️ What this means in practice

When designing your training, ask:

- Where will learners practice?  
- How will they receive feedback?  
- How will they improve their work?  
- How will I support iteration?  

---

## Common mistakes

- Including too little practice  
- Providing vague or delayed feedback  
- Not allowing time for revision  
- Relying only on facilitator feedback  
- Treating training as a one-time event
