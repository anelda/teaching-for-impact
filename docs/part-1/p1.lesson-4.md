## Why this matters

In the previous section, you explored how people learn and what supports effective learning.

This section focuses on making your training purposeful and aligned—so that learning leads to meaningful outcomes.

Training often fails not because of poor delivery, but because:

- outcomes are unclear  
- activities do not support what learners need to do  
- success is not defined or measured meaningfully  

As a result, participants may engage with the training but struggle to apply what they learned.

Effective training requires alignment between:

- what learners should be able to do  
- what they do during training  
- how success is recognised  

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“What should I include?”*  
    to:  
    *“What should learners be able to do—and how will I support and recognise that?”*

---

## Core concepts

### Outcomes, outputs, and impact

!!! abstract "Outcomes, outputs, and impact"
    Outputs are immediate results of training, outcomes are what learners can do, and impact is the longer-term change that results.

- **Outputs** — completed activities (e.g. a worksheet, a discussion)  
- **Outcomes** — what learners can do after training  
- **Impact** — what changes as a result in the real world  

Design implication: focus your design on outcomes, not just outputs.

---

### Backward design

!!! abstract "Backward design"
    Designing training by starting with outcomes, then defining evidence, and only then designing activities.

This follows a simple sequence:

- Outcomes → Evidence → Activities  

Design implication: do not start with content—start with what learners need to do.

---

### Constructive alignment

Constructive alignment ensures that:

- outcomes, activities, and assessment all support each other  

This means:

- activities help learners achieve outcomes  
- assessment reflects those outcomes  

Design implication: every activity should have a clear purpose linked to an outcome.

---

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Define intended outcomes

Be specific about what learners should be able to do.

Strong outcomes:

- are observable  
- involve action  
- reflect real-world use  

For example:

- “Interpret climate data to identify local risks”  
- “Use a tool to analyse and communicate findings”  

---

### Step 2 — Define evidence of success

Ask:

- What would demonstrate that learning has happened?  

Focus on:

- observable actions  
- meaningful outputs  
- decision-making or application  

Keep this simple:

- 2–3 clear indicators are enough  

---

### Step 3 — Design aligned activities

For each outcome, design activities where learners:

- practice the skill  
- apply knowledge  
- receive feedback  

Check:

- Does this activity help achieve the outcome?  

---

### Step 4 — Check alignment

Review your design:

- What is taught but not assessed?  
- What is assessed but not practiced?  
- Which activities do not contribute to outcomes?  

---

### Step 5 — Ensure contextual relevance

Make sure outcomes reflect:

- what learners will actually need to do  
- the constraints they operate under  
- the decisions they will face  

---

### Decision point

> What choice does the reader need to make here?

> How specific and practice-oriented will your outcomes be?

- Broad and general (easier to write, harder to apply)  
- Moderately specific (some application possible)  
- Highly specific and action-oriented (clear application and assessment)  

Your choice will determine how useful and transferable your training is.

---

## Example

**Context:**  
A team is designing training for early-career researchers on using climate data tools.

**Decision:**  
Should they define outcomes broadly (“understand climate data tools”) or specifically (“use a tool to analyse and interpret local data”)?

**Action:**  
They choose to:

- define specific, actionable outcomes  
- identify evidence (e.g. completing an analysis task)  
- design activities where learners use the tool in context  

They also remove activities that do not support these outcomes.

**Outcome:**  
Learners are able to apply the tool in their own work, and facilitators can clearly recognise whether learning has occurred.

    Start with what learners need to do—then design everything else to support that.
