## Why this matters

Training is often designed:

- by experts  
- for learners  
- without meaningful input from those it aims to support  

This can lead to:

- low relevance  
- poor uptake  
- solutions that do not fit real contexts  

At the same time, many training contexts are **resource-constrained**:

- limited time  
- limited technology  
- limited funding  
- complex social and institutional dynamics  

Effective training requires:

- **working with people, not for them**
- **designing within real constraints, not ideal conditions**

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *“What would be ideal?”*  

    to:

    *“What is possible, meaningful, and sustainable in this context?”*

---

!!! abstract "Co-design"
    A participatory approach where learners or stakeholders actively contribute to shaping the training.

!!! abstract "Constraints"
    Real-world limits (time, resources, access, social dynamics) that shape what is possible.

---

!!! tip "Key takeaway"  
    Design with people and within constraints—not for ideal conditions.

---

## What is co-design?

Co-design is a **participatory approach to design** where:

- stakeholders (including learners and communities)  
- actively contribute to shaping the training  

This includes:

- defining problems  
- setting priorities  
- designing activities  
- refining solutions  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Including participants without giving them real influence (tokenism).

---

### Co-design vs traditional design

| Traditional approach | Co-design approach |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Designed by experts | Designed with participants |
| Fixed content | Adaptive and iterative |
| Learners as recipients | Learners as contributors |

---

### Why co-design matters

Co-design:

- increases relevance  
- improves engagement  
- surfaces local knowledge  
- supports ownership and sustainability  

---

## What co-design looks like in practice

Co-design does not need to be complex.

It can include:

- discussing needs with participants before designing  
- adapting content based on feedback  
- inviting participants to shape activities  
- iterating based on real use  

---

### Examples

- Co-defining learning goals with participants  
- Using participant examples and case studies  
- Testing and refining activities together  
- Incorporating feedback into future iterations  

---

## Constraints are not a problem—they are part of the design

Constraints include:

- Technology (limited devices, connectivity)  
- Time (short sessions, competing priorities)  
- Resources (materials, funding, infrastructure)  
- Language and literacy  
- Social and cultural dynamics  

---

### Why this matters

Designing for ideal conditions often leads to:

- solutions that cannot be implemented  
- frustration for participants  

Designing within constraints leads to:

- practical and usable solutions  

---

## Designing for low-resource contexts

In many settings, you may need to:

- use low-bandwidth or offline approaches  
- design for shared devices  
- simplify materials and instructions  
- prioritise essential content  
- use locally available resources  

---

### ⚙️ Practical strategies

- “Offline-first” design (materials usable without internet)  
- Short, focused activities instead of long sessions  
- Use of simple tools (paper, discussion, local examples)  
- Flexible delivery formats  

---

## Trade-offs and prioritisation

You cannot optimise everything.

You will need to make trade-offs between:

- depth vs breadth  
- interactivity vs time  
- complexity vs accessibility  

---

### Key question

> What matters most for learning in this context?

---

### ⚙️ Practical approach

- Identify your most important outcomes  
- Prioritise activities that support those outcomes  
- Remove or simplify non-essential elements  

---

## Avoiding extractive practices

!!! abstract "Extractive practice"
    Using participants’ knowledge or input without meaningful benefit to them.

In some cases, training can unintentionally become:

- extractive (taking knowledge without giving value)  
- tokenistic (including participants without real influence)  

---

### Signs of extractive practice

- Participants provide input but do not influence decisions  
- Local knowledge is used but not acknowledged  
- Benefits do not flow back to participants or communities  

---

### Principles for ethical co-design

- **Reciprocity** — participants benefit from the process  
- **Transparency** — decisions and intentions are clear  
- **Respect** — local knowledge is valued  
- **Shared ownership** — participants influence outcomes  

---

!!! warning "Common mistake"
    Using local knowledge without acknowledging or benefiting participants.

---

## Power and decision-making

Co-design requires attention to:

- who makes decisions  
- whose voices are heard  
- who is excluded  

---

### Practical questions

- Who currently decides what is taught?  
- Who should be involved in these decisions?  
- What barriers prevent participation in design?

## Iterating with communities

Co-design is not a one-time activity.

It involves:

- testing ideas  
- gathering feedback  
- adapting based on real use  

---

### Simple iteration cycle

1. Design with initial input  
2. Test with participants  
3. Gather feedback  
4. Adapt and improve  

---

## What this means in practice

Before finalising your training, you should be able to answer:

- Who has shaped this training so far?
- Whose voices are missing?
- What constraints must I design for?
- What trade-offs am I making?
- How will I involve participants in improving this?

---

## Common mistakes

- Designing for ideal conditions instead of real contexts  
- Treating co-design as a one-off consultation  
- Ignoring power dynamics  
- Trying to include everything instead of prioritising  
- Creating solutions that cannot be sustained
