## Why this matters

You have now:

- defined your training as an intervention  
- understood your learners and context  
- designed outcomes and activities  
- built in practice, feedback, and evaluation  

The next step is to consider:

> Can your training be used again—by you or someone else—without starting from scratch?  
> And do you need to create new materials at all, or could you reuse or adapt what already exists?

This is where **Open Educational Resources (OER)** become important.

---

## What is the purpose of this section?

This is a **bridge**, not a deep dive.

!!! quote "▶️ This section helps you move from..."

    *designing training for a single delivery*  

    to:

    *designing training that you can reuse, adapt, and share*

---

## What are Open Educational Resources (OER)?

Open Educational Resources are:

- teaching and learning materials  
- that are freely available  
- and can be reused, adapted, and shared  

---

### In practical terms

OER means materials that:

- you can reuse in future  
- others can understand and use  
- can be adapted to different contexts  

---

## Start with your own benefit

Before thinking about others, consider this:

> You are very likely to reuse your own training.

You may need to:

- run the same training again  
- adapt it for a different audience  
- scale it to new locations or programmes  
- update and improve it over time  

If your materials are not:

- clearly structured  
- easy to understand  
- designed for adaptation  

...you will need to:

- rebuild or heavily revise them each time  

---

### What changes when you design for reuse

Designing with reuse in mind helps you:

- **Save time** — you don’t need to recreate materials  
- **Adapt quickly** — you can adjust for new contexts  
- **Improve quality** — each version builds on the previous one  
- **Reduce effort over time** — less duplication  
- **Maintain consistency** — across different runs  

---

### Key shift

> You are designing for your *future self* as much as for others. Your future self will not remember all the details — your materials need to carry that knowledge.

---

## Reuse or create? (a critical decision)

Before developing new materials, ask:

> Do I need to create this, or can I reuse something that already exists?

In practice, you have three options:

- **Reuse** — use an existing resource as-is  
- **Adapt** — modify an existing resource to fit your context  
- **Create** — develop something new  

---

### In reality, you will often combine approaches

Most training is not built using only one approach.

You may:

- reuse an existing activity  
- adapt examples or instructions  
- create new content where nothing suitable exists  

> Effective training design often involves a **combination of reuse, adaptation, and creation**.

---

### Why this matters

Creating from scratch:

- takes time  
- duplicates effort  
- may not lead to better results  

Reusing or adapting:

- can save significant time  
- builds on existing knowledge  
- allows you to focus effort where it matters most  

---

### But reuse is not always easier

Finding and adapting materials:

- takes time  
- requires judgement  
- may involve trade-offs  

---

### Practical approach

Do not assume one approach is better.

Instead, decide based on:

- relevance to your outcomes  
- effort required to adapt  
- fit with your context  

---

### Key shift

> Do not start from zero by default.  
> Start by checking what already exists.

---

## Why OER still matters beyond you

Once your materials are:

- reusable  
- understandable  
- adaptable  

...it becomes easy to:

- share them  
- support others  
- extend impact  

---

## The shift: from delivery to reuse

Designing for delivery focuses on:

- your session  
- your participants  
- your facilitation  

Designing for reuse means thinking about:

- future versions of your training  
- different audiences  
- different contexts  

---

### Key question

> Can this training work again—without needing to redesign it or rely on your memory of how it was delivered?

---

## What makes training reusable?

For your materials to be reusable (by you or others), they need to be:

---

### 1. Understandable

- clear instructions  
- simple structure  
- minimal reliance on explanation  

---

### 2. Adaptable

- not overly tied to one context  
- flexible and editable  
- examples that can be replaced  

---

### 3. Accessible

- usable with limited technology  
- readable and clear  
- appropriate for different environments  

---

### 4. Documented

- explains how to use the materials  
- includes context and purpose  
- provides guidance for facilitators  

---

## Hidden assumptions (critical concept)

Most training materials contain **implicit knowledge**, such as:

- background explanations not written down  
- assumptions about tools or skills  
- contextual or cultural expectations  

---

### Why this matters

These assumptions:

- make materials hard for others to use  
- make it harder for *you* to reuse them later  

---

### Practical approach

Ask:

- What does someone need to know to use this?  
- What have I not written down?  

---

## Introducing openness (light touch)

Once your materials are reusable, the next step is to make them **open**.

This means:

- allowing reuse  
- allowing adaptation  
- making permissions clear  

👉 You will explore this further in Part II.

---

## Designing with reuse in mind (practical decisions)

You do not need to redesign everything.

Start with small changes:

- simplify instructions  
- remove unnecessary context  
- add missing explanations  
- structure materials clearly  
- make activities self-contained  

---

## Reuse as a design constraint

Instead of treating OER as an add-on, ask:

- Can I use this again easily?  
- Can I adapt this without starting over?  
- What would someone else struggle with?  

---

## What this means in practice

Before moving to OER-focused sections, you should be able to answer:

- Can I reuse this training without redesigning it?  
- Should I reuse, adapt, or create new materials?  
- Where can I combine these approaches effectively?  
- What assumptions are embedded in my materials?  
- What would need to change for a different context?  

---

!!! warning "Common mistakes"

    - Designing only for a single delivery  
    - Starting from scratch without checking existing resources  
    - Treating reuse, adaptation, and creation as separate instead of combined  
    - Assuming materials will be easy to reuse later  
    - Including too much context-specific detail  
    - Not documenting how materials are used  
    - Treating openness as a final step

## Optional deeper insight

This section draws on:

- Open Education and OER frameworks  
- Creative Commons licensing principles  
- Open Science and knowledge sharing practices  

These perspectives emphasise that:

- knowledge is often already available and can be reused  
- adaptation is a core part of how knowledge travels across contexts  
- combining reuse, adaptation, and creation leads to more efficient and effective design  
- designing for reuse benefits both individual practitioners and wider communities  

In practice, this means:

- you do not need to work in isolation  
- your work can build on what exists  
- and others can build on yours in return
